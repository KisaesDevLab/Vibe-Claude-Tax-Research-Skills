# §183 nine factors — case-law touchstones

Treas. Reg. § 1.183-2(b) lists nine factors for the for-profit
inquiry. The list is non-exhaustive and no factor is determinative.
This reference catalogs leading cases by factor pattern.

## Factor 1 — Manner in which activity carried on

- Engdahl v. Commissioner, 72 T.C. 659 (1979) — businesslike
  records, separate bank account, written business plan supported
  for-profit conclusion despite losses.
- Dodge v. Commissioner, T.C. Memo. 1998-89 — failure to maintain
  books and records weighed against for-profit motive.

## Factor 2 — Expertise

- Burger v. Commissioner, T.C. Memo. 2007-198 — taxpayer's
  professional consultation supported for-profit motive.

## Factor 3 — Time and effort

- Hastings v. Commissioner, T.C. Memo. 2002-310 — minimal time
  weighed against for-profit; offset only by hiring competent
  qualified persons.

## Factor 4 — Asset appreciation expectation

- Faulconer v. Commissioner, 748 F.2d 890 (4th Cir. 1984) —
  appreciation of breeding stock supported for-profit motive even
  with operating losses.

## Factor 5 — Success in other activities

- Helmick v. Commissioner, T.C. Memo. 2009-220 — taxpayer's
  unrelated business success can support inference of profit motive.

## Factor 6 — History of losses

- Burger v. Commissioner — start-up losses tolerated; long-term
  losses without trajectory toward profitability weighed against.

## Factor 7 — Occasional profits

- Magassy v. Commissioner, T.C. Memo. 2004-4 — occasional small
  profits compared with substantial losses cut against for-profit.

## Factor 8 — Financial status

- Wesinger v. Commissioner, T.C. Memo. 1999-372 — high other income
  raises inference of personal-pleasure motive.

## Factor 9 — Personal pleasure / recreation

- Hendricks v. Commissioner, 32 F.3d 94 (4th Cir. 1994) —
  recreational character (e.g., car racing) does not preclude
  for-profit motive but raises scrutiny.
- Donoghue v. Commissioner, T.C. Memo. 2019-71 — boat charter with
  significant personal use weighed heavily against for-profit.

## Specific activity types

### Horse breeding / racing
- §183(d) special rule: 2 of 7 years presumption.
- Engdahl, supra; Faulconer, supra.

### Yacht / boat charter
- Donoghue, supra; Wesinger, supra.
- Personal-pleasure factor (factor 9) typically dominant.

### Art / writing / photography
- Churchman v. Commissioner, 68 T.C. 696 (1977) — fine artist
  recognized as for-profit despite years of losses (factor 4
  appreciation; factor 2 expertise).

### Day trading / investing
- Mayer v. Commissioner, T.C. Memo. 1994-209 — distinguishes trader
  (§ 162) from investor (§ 212) status; "trader" status requires
  substantial, continuous, regular activity.

## Relationship to §183(d) presumption

§183(d) provides:
- 3 of 5 consecutive years gross income > deductions: presumption
  for-profit.
- 2 of 7 for horse-related activities.
- Form 5213 election can postpone the §183(d) determination until
  after the presumption period expires.

When the presumption is met, the IRS bears the burden of proving
the activity is not for-profit. Cite Treas. Reg. § 1.183-2.
