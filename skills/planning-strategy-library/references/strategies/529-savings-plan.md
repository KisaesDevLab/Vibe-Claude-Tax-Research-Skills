## Overview

§529 Qualified Tuition Programs (QTPs), commonly called 529 plans,
are state-sponsored or qualified educational institution programs
that allow tax-advantaged saving for educational expenses:

1. **Contributions** — not federally deductible (some states allow
   state income tax deduction or credit for contributions to home-
   state plan).

2. **Tax-free growth** — earnings accumulate without federal income
   tax.

3. **Tax-free distributions** for qualified educational expenses —
   tuition, fees, books, supplies, equipment, room and board (for
   at least half-time students), computer equipment.

4. **K-12 expenses** (post-TCJA) — up to $10,000 per beneficiary
   per year for tuition at elementary or secondary public, private,
   or religious school.

5. **Apprenticeship programs** (post-SECURE Act) — qualified
   apprenticeship programs registered with DOL.

6. **Student loan repayment** (post-SECURE Act) — up to $10,000
   lifetime per beneficiary for qualified education loans.

7. **Roth IRA rollover** (SECURE 2.0, effective 2024) — up to
   $35,000 lifetime per beneficiary, subject to conditions: 529
   account must have been maintained 15+ years; rollover ≤ annual
   Roth IRA contribution limit; beneficiary must have earned
   income.

Contribution mechanics:
- **No federal contribution limit** — state plans set aggregate
  limits ($300K-$550K typical).
- **Annual exclusion** — contributions are gifts subject to §2503(b)
  annual exclusion ($18,000/donor/donee 2024).
- **5-year forward gift election** — §529(c)(2)(B) — fund 5 years'
  worth of annual exclusion in one year ($90,000 single / $180,000
  MFJ 2024); ratable over 5 years for gift tax.
- **Change of beneficiary** — to family members without tax
  consequence.

Plan types:
- **Education Savings Plans (most common)** — investment account;
  performance-based; donor chooses portfolio.
- **Prepaid Tuition Plans** — locks in tuition at today's rates;
  more limited; some state plans.

§529A ABLE accounts (separate but parallel) — for individuals with
disabilities; up to $18,000/year (2024) annual contributions.

## Eligibility requirements

For tax-free distributions:
1. Distribution used for qualified higher education expenses (or
   qualified K-12 / apprenticeship / student loan).
2. Beneficiary is taxpayer or family member.
3. Distribution does not exceed qualified expenses for the year.

For Roth IRA rollover:
1. 529 account maintained ≥15 years.
2. Rollover ≤ annual Roth IRA contribution limit ($7,000 for 2024
   under-50).
3. Beneficiary has earned income.
4. ≤$35,000 lifetime per beneficiary.

## Mechanics — how it works

1. **Open 529 account** with state plan or institution.
2. **Contribute funds.**
3. **Investment growth** — tax-deferred at federal level; state
   tax treatment varies.
4. **Distribution for qualified expenses.**
5. **Form 1099-Q** issued by plan; reconcile with qualified
   expenses on tax return.
6. **State income tax** — verify state deduction or credit for
   contributions (in-state plan typically required).
7. **Coordinate with §25A education credits** — cannot use same
   expenses for §529 distribution exclusion AND education credit
   (double-dipping prohibited).

## Documentation requirements

- Form 1099-Q (each year of distribution).
- Receipts for qualified educational expenses.
- 529 statements.
- For Roth IRA rollover: 15-year account history; beneficiary
  earned income.
- Form 709 if 5-year forward gift election used.

## Common pitfalls / audit risks

- **Non-qualified distributions.** Earnings portion subject to
  ordinary income tax + 10% penalty. Principal portion not
  taxable.
- **Double-dipping with education credits.** Same expenses cannot
  be used for both §529 exclusion and §25A credit.
- **Excess contribution.** State aggregate limits; gift tax issues.
- **State plan vs. another state's plan.** State deduction usually
  requires home-state plan.
- **K-12 limit.** $10,000/year per beneficiary for K-12 tuition.
- **Roth rollover restrictions.** 15-year aging period;
  earnings-test limitations.
- **Beneficiary not in qualified education.** Distributions to
  non-student beneficiary trigger penalty.

## State conformity considerations

State 529 deductions vary widely:
- **Most states** allow deduction or credit for contributions to
  in-state plan only.
- **Some states (Arizona, Kansas, Maine, Missouri, Pennsylvania)**
  allow deduction for contributions to any state's plan.
- **A few states** offer matching grants for low-income
  participants.
- **State plans differ in fees, investment options, and aggregate
  limits.**
