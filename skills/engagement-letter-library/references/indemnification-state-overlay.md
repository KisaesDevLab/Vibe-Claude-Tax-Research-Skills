# Indemnification / limitation of liability — state overlay

Engagement-letter risk-allocation provisions vary in enforceability
by state and by engagement type. Attest engagements are the
strictest; indemnification of the firm by the client for the
firm's own acts is restricted by both state law AND AICPA Ethics
Interpretation §1.400.205 (independence implications).

## AICPA Ethics Interpretation §1.400.205

For ATTEST engagements, indemnification provisions in the
engagement letter that protect the firm from its own acts (other
than those involving knowing misrepresentation by the client) may
be evaluated as creating a self-interest threat to independence.

Permitted (independence not impaired):
- Indemnification by the client for liabilities arising from
  client's knowing misrepresentations to the firm.
- Indemnification for the firm's costs of responding to subpoenas
  related to the engagement (where the firm is not a target).

Generally NOT permitted on attest engagements (independence
implications):
- Indemnification for the firm's gross negligence or willful
  misconduct.
- Limitation of firm liability to the engagement fee for the
  firm's own acts.
- Hold-harmless clauses covering firm's professional acts.

For NON-ATTEST engagements (tax, advisory, bookkeeping),
indemnification is more often permissible but state-by-state
enforceability still varies.

## State overlay — known stricter

### California (Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code §5063.1)

For attest engagements:
- Indemnification clauses for the firm's own acts are LIMITED.
- Limitation-of-liability clauses are restricted.
- Firm cannot disclaim liability for negligence on attest
  engagements.

For non-attest engagements:
- Limitation of liability typically permitted but should be
  reasonable in light of fees and exposure.

### Illinois (similar restrictions on attest)
- Limitation of liability on attest engagements is generally not
  enforceable.
- Caps on liability for non-attest may be enforceable if reasonable.

### New Jersey
- Restrictions on indemnification provisions on attest engagements
  consistent with AICPA Interpretation.
- Limitation of liability on attest engagements: scrutinized.

### Texas (22 TAC §501.71)
- General contract law applies.
- Public-policy review of unconscionable provisions.
- Indemnification on attest engagements should follow AICPA
  Ethics Interpretation.

### New York
- General contract law applies.
- Public-policy review.
- AICPA Ethics Interpretation §1.400.205 followed by state board.

## Sample language (non-attest engagements)

For non-attest engagements (where state law permits and firm
counsel approves):

### Limitation of liability (non-attest, typical)
> "To the maximum extent permitted by [STATE] law, the firm's
> aggregate liability arising from this engagement is limited to
> [MULTIPLE OF FEES OR DOLLAR AMOUNT]. This limitation does not
> apply to liability arising from the firm's gross negligence,
> willful misconduct, or fraud."

### Indemnification — narrow (non-attest, knowing
misrepresentation)
> "Client agrees to indemnify and hold the firm harmless from
> liabilities, costs, and expenses arising from client's knowing
> or grossly negligent misrepresentations to the firm. This
> indemnification does not extend to the firm's own acts."

## Sample language (attest engagements)

For attest engagements, AICPA Ethics Interpretation §1.400.205 +
state overlay restrict indemnification. Recommended approach:

### Limited risk allocation (attest, AICPA-compliant)
> "The firm will perform this engagement in accordance with
> applicable AICPA standards and exercise the professional care
> and judgment expected of a reasonably prudent practitioner.
> The firm's liability for any matter arising out of this
> engagement is governed by applicable law and AICPA Ethics
> Interpretation §1.400.205. Indemnification provisions favoring
> the firm for the firm's own acts are not included on attest
> engagements consistent with the AICPA's independence-impairment
> guidance."

For client misrepresentation:
> "Client represents that all information provided to the firm
> is accurate and complete. Client agrees to indemnify the firm
> from liabilities arising from client's knowing or grossly
> negligent misrepresentations."

## Insurance and financial responsibility

The engagement letter may reference firm's professional liability
insurance:
> "The firm maintains professional liability insurance in
> amounts consistent with industry standards. Coverage details
> available upon request."

For high-dollar attest engagements, the engagement letter does
NOT need to disclose specific coverage amounts but the firm
should ensure coverage is appropriate to engagement risk.

## Mediation / arbitration interaction

Pre-dispute mandatory arbitration on attest engagements is
generally enforceable but some states impose disclosure
requirements (CA, IL). Firm counsel should confirm enforceability
in `engagement_state`.

## Alternative dispute resolution language

> "The parties agree to submit any dispute arising from this
> engagement first to mediation in good faith for [NN] days. If
> not resolved, the parties may pursue binding arbitration under
> [AAA Commercial Arbitration Rules / OTHER PROVIDER] in
> [LOCATION]. Disputes related to fees less than [$AMOUNT] may
> be brought in [COUNTY] small claims court."

## Practitioner risk-management notes

- High-dollar engagements (audit of large entities, complex SOC
  examinations, multi-jurisdictional tax engagements):
  professional liability insurance review with broker BEFORE
  signing engagement letter.
- Litigation-support engagements: advocacy threat under §1.295;
  consider declining if independence will be required for the
  same client.
- Successor-auditor engagements: predecessor-auditor communication
  per AU-C §510 and AR-C §510-equivalent (where applicable);
  firm counsel review of any indemnification negotiated with
  predecessor.
