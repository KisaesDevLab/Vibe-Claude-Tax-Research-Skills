# Multi-circuit factor lists for debt vs equity

Identify the taxpayer's binding circuit before applying a factor
list (Golsen rule).

## 5th Cir. — Estate of Mixon (13 factors)

Estate of Mixon v. United States, 464 F.2d 394 (5th Cir. 1972):

1. The name given to the certificates.
2. The presence or absence of a fixed maturity date.
3. The source of payments.
4. The right to enforce payment of principal and interest.
5. Participation in management resulting from the advances.
6. Status of the advances in relation to regular corporate
   creditors.
7. The intent of the parties.
8. Identity of interest between creditor and stockholder.
9. "Thin" or adequate capitalization.
10. Ability of corporation to obtain loans from outside lending
    institutions.
11. The extent to which the advances were used to acquire capital
    assets.
12. The failure of the debtor to repay on the due date or to seek
    a postponement.
13. The risk involved.

## 6th Cir. — Roth Steel / Indmar (11 factors)

Roth Steel Tube Co. v. Commissioner, 800 F.2d 625 (6th Cir. 1986);
Indmar Products Co. v. Commissioner, 444 F.3d 771 (6th Cir. 2006):

1. The names given to the instruments.
2. The presence or absence of a fixed maturity date.
3. The source of the payments.
4. The right to enforce payment.
5. Participation in management.
6. Status equal to or inferior to that of regular corporate
   creditors.
7. The intent of the parties.
8. "Thin" or adequate capitalization.
9. Identity of interest between creditor and stockholder.
10. Source of interest payments.
11. Ability of corporation to obtain loans from outside lending
    institutions.

## 11th Cir. — Slappey Drive (13 factors, derived from 5th Cir.)

Slappey Drive Indus. Park v. United States, 561 F.2d 572 (5th Cir.
1977) — adopted by 11th Cir. as binding under Bonner.

## 9th Cir. — Hardman / multi-factor

Hardman v. United States, 827 F.2d 1409 (9th Cir. 1987) — applies
a similar multi-factor test with emphasis on subjective intent and
objective economic reality.

## Other circuits

- 2d Cir.: Gilbert v. Commissioner, 248 F.2d 399 (2d Cir. 1957) —
  classic restated analysis.
- 3d Cir.: similar multi-factor; emphasis on capitalization.
- 7th Cir.: emphasis on objective economic indicia.

The factor lists have substantial overlap; the practitioner must
confirm the specific list and emphasis applied in the binding
circuit.

## §385 final regulations (limited impact)

§1.385-1 — definitions and General Rule (consistency between
issuer and holder under §385(c) preserved).

§1.385-2 — documentation requirements:
- Originally effective for "expanded-group instruments" issued on
  or after 1/1/2018.
- Notice 2017-36 deferred application.
- Final regulations were never enforced as initially proposed;
  proposed withdrawal in 2018; further delays make the
  documentation rules largely dormant in practice.
- Verify current status via the Federal Register IRS feed before
  relying on the documentation-rule framework.

§1.385-3 / -3T — covered-debt-instrument anti-avoidance:
- Targets debt issued by a U.S. member of an expanded affiliated
  group in connection with funded acquisitions or distributions.
- Per-issuer threshold $50M aggregate.
- Anti-cascading and anti-circumvention rules.
- Recharacterizes covered instruments as stock for federal tax
  purposes.

The §1.385-3 rules apply primarily to multinational corporate
groups; small / closely-held cases are governed almost entirely by
the common-law factor analysis.

## Below-market loans (§7872)

If a shareholder loan to a corporation is made at below-market
rate (AFR), §7872 imputes interest. §1274 covers original-issue-
discount on certain other below-market debt. These provisions
operate in parallel to debt-vs-equity characterization and may
result in a recharacterized "loan" that is ALSO a deemed-interest-
income / dividend-and-recontribution flow.

## Common closely-held fact patterns

### Open advances treated as debt
- Documented promissory note with stated maturity and AFR rate.
- Quarterly or annual interest payments actually made.
- Repayments scheduled or made on a regular basis.
- Corporate board resolution.

### Open advances likely recharacterized as equity
- "Loan" recorded only on the books; no note.
- Interest accrued but never paid.
- Repayment "as available" / "as cash flow permits".
- Loans made when the corporation cannot borrow externally.
- Capitalization ratio > 3:1 debt to equity (rough threshold).
- Identity of holdings between equity and debt closely matched.

## Estate-tax interactions

If the lender dies, the loan's character matters for estate tax.
A debt instrument is includible at face plus accrued interest; an
equity interest is includible at FMV per §2031 / §2033. Document
contemporaneously to support the chosen characterization.

## Practitioner audit-defense checklist

- [ ] Written promissory note with specified maturity, rate (≥
  AFR), payment schedule.
- [ ] Board resolution authorizing the loan.
- [ ] Security agreement and UCC filings if collateralized.
- [ ] Actual cash interest payments documented; cancelled checks
  or wire confirmations.
- [ ] Repayments tracked separately from distributions.
- [ ] Corporation's debt-to-equity ratio computed; if > 3:1, document
  the operational rationale and external creditor analysis.
- [ ] If shareholder advances exceed market debt-financing capacity,
  partial recharacterization risk acknowledged.
- [ ] Issuer's books and Forms 1120 / 1120-S / 1065 consistent with
  the holder's Forms 1099-INT or income reporting (§385(c)(1)).
