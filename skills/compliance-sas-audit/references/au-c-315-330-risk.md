# AU-C 315 / 330 — Risk Assessment and Response

AU-C 315 establishes the auditor's responsibility to identify and
assess the risks of material misstatement (RMM). AU-C 330 then
specifies the auditor's response. Together, they form the spine
of the audit-risk model.

## AU-C 315 — Identifying and assessing RMM

### Understanding the entity (AU-C §315.13–.21)

The auditor obtains an understanding of:
- Industry, regulatory, and other external factors.
- Nature of the entity (operations, ownership, governance,
  investments, structure, financing).
- Selection and application of accounting policies.
- Objectives, strategies, and related business risks.
- Measurement and review of financial performance.

### Understanding internal control (AU-C §315.21+)

For each component of ICFR:
- Control environment.
- Risk assessment.
- Information system, including business processes relevant to
  financial reporting.
- Control activities.
- Monitoring of controls.

The auditor evaluates the design of controls and determines whether
they have been implemented (D&I). Operating effectiveness is tested
in AU-C 330 if the auditor plans to rely on controls.

### Identifying RMM (AU-C §315.26+)

- At the F/S level.
- At the assertion level (for classes of transactions, account
  balances, disclosures).
- Significant risks (require special audit consideration).

### Significant risks (AU-C §315.27)

A risk is significant when:
- It involves significant judgment.
- Relates to recent significant economic, accounting, or other
  developments and warrants special attention.
- Involves complex transactions.
- Involves significant transactions with related parties.
- Involves a high degree of subjectivity.
- Is presumptively present (revenue-recognition fraud — AU-C 240;
  management override — AU-C 240).

For significant risks, the auditor:
- Obtains an understanding of relevant controls.
- Designs further audit procedures specifically responsive to the
  risk.
- Performs tests of details (controls testing alone is
  insufficient).
- Documents the risk and the rationale.

### Risk-assessment procedures (AU-C §315.06)

- Inquiries of management and others (internal audit, governance,
  in-house counsel, IT).
- Analytical procedures.
- Observation and inspection.

The auditor's risk assessment is iterative — updated as the audit
progresses and new information becomes available.

## AU-C 330 — The auditor's response

### Overall responses (AU-C §330.05)

- Assignment / supervision of personnel.
- Skepticism level appropriate to the engagement.
- Predictability / unpredictability in procedures.

### Audit procedures responsive to assessed RMM (AU-C §330.06–.18)

#### Tests of controls
- Performed when:
  - The auditor's risk assessment includes an expectation that
    controls operate effectively, AND
  - Substantive procedures alone cannot provide sufficient evidence
    at the assertion level (e.g., automated transaction processing
    where substantive testing is impractical).
- Operating-effectiveness testing.

#### Substantive procedures
- Tests of details.
- Substantive analytical procedures (where the relationship is
  predictable, the data is reliable, and the level of assurance
  needed is moderate).

#### Required substantive procedures (AU-C §330.18)
- Agreeing or reconciling F/S to the underlying accounting records.
- Examining material journal entries and other adjustments made
  during the course of preparing the F/S.

### Tests of controls and reliance

If the auditor relies on controls operating effectively:
- Test the operating effectiveness of those controls.
- For controls over significant risks: test in the period under
  audit (cannot rely solely on prior-period testing).
- For other controls: rotation testing may be appropriate (test
  every 2nd or 3rd year, with annual updating procedures).

### Substantive procedures for significant risks (AU-C §330.21)

For each significant risk, perform substantive procedures
specifically responsive to the risk. Tests of details are
required.

### Adequacy of presentation and disclosure (AU-C §330.24)

Perform audit procedures to evaluate whether the overall
presentation, structure, and content of the F/S, including the
related disclosures, conform to the framework.

## Materiality (AU-C 320 — sister section)

Materiality is determined at three levels:
- Materiality for the F/S as a whole.
- Performance materiality (the amount set by the auditor at less
  than overall materiality to reduce to an appropriately low
  level the probability that the aggregate of uncorrected and
  undetected misstatements exceeds materiality for the F/S as a
  whole).
- Materiality for particular classes of transactions, account
  balances, or disclosures (lower than overall materiality if
  necessary).

Common starting points (industry-dependent; not authority — these
are practitioner conventions):
- Pre-tax income: 5% (volatile entities) to 10% (stable
  entities).
- Total assets / revenue: 0.5–1%.
- Member equity / contributed capital (entities without P&L
  focus).

## Documentation (AU-C §315.32 / §330.30)

- Discussion among engagement team.
- Key elements of the understanding obtained.
- Sources of information used.
- Risk assessment procedures performed.
- Identified and assessed RMM.
- Significant risks.
- Risks for which substantive procedures alone cannot provide
  sufficient evidence.
- Overall responses.
- Tests of controls (nature, timing, extent, results, conclusions).
- Substantive procedures (nature, timing, extent, results,
  conclusions).
