# §1031 timelines, traps, and computational mechanics

## 45-day identification

§1031(a)(3)(A) and Treas. Reg. §1.1031(k)-1(b)(2). Replacement
property must be identified in writing, signed by the taxpayer,
and delivered to the QI (or other party other than the taxpayer
or a disqualified person) within 45 days after the transfer of
the relinquished property.

Identification is permitted under any one of three rules
(§1.1031(k)-1(c)(4)):
- **Three-property rule**: any three properties regardless of FMV.
- **200% rule**: any number of properties, aggregate FMV ≤ 200% of
  the FMV of all relinquished properties.
- **95% rule**: any number of properties (FMV unrestricted), but
  taxpayer must actually receive at least 95% of the aggregate
  FMV identified.

Replacement property must be unambiguously described — legal
description, street address, or distinguishable name preferred.

## 180-day completion

§1031(a)(3)(B). Receipt of replacement property must occur within
the EARLIER of:
- 180 days after the transfer of the relinquished property; OR
- The due date (with extensions) of the taxpayer's return for the
  taxable year in which the transfer occurred.

Practitioners with clients exchanging in November–December often
need to file an extension to capture the full 180-day window
without the return-due-date cap.

## Disaster-relief extensions

Notice-based extensions (e.g., Rev. Proc. 2018-58, §17) extend
both 45- and 180-day deadlines for taxpayers in
presidentially-declared disaster areas. Verify the specific Notice
or extending authority for the disaster in question.

## Disqualified persons (§1.1031(k)-1(k))

A person who:
- Is the taxpayer's agent (employee, attorney, accountant,
  investment banker / broker, or real estate agent / broker) at
  the time of the transaction; OR has been within the 2-year
  period ending on the date of transfer; OR
- Is related to the taxpayer under §267(b) (with > 10% threshold
  substituted for > 50%) or §707(b); OR
- Bears a relationship to the taxpayer's agent that would
  disqualify the agent.

Performance of routine financial, title insurance, escrow, or
trust services for the taxpayer does not by itself create
disqualification.

## Qualified Intermediary (QI) safe harbor

§1.1031(k)-1(g)(4):
- Written exchange agreement.
- QI takes assignment of the contract for the relinquished
  property and the contract for the replacement property.
- QI receives proceeds from buyer of relinquished property and
  uses them to acquire replacement property; taxpayer never has
  receipt or control.
- QI's holding restricted to: pay transactional costs, return
  excess to taxpayer (boot), or fund replacement.
- "(g)(6) limitations" restrict the taxpayer's rights during the
  exchange period (no receive / pledge / borrow / hypothecate,
  except after the 45-day window in narrow circumstances).

## Constructive receipt

§1.1031(k)-1(f). If at any time during the exchange period the
taxpayer has unrestricted access to the proceeds, §1031 is voided.
This is the most common failure mode in DIY exchanges.

## Related-party rule (§1031(f))

§1031(f)(1): related-party exchange (§267(b) / §707(b)) requires
both parties to hold for ≥ 2 years. Disposition before the 2-year
mark triggers retroactive gain recognition for the taxpayer.

§1031(f)(2) exceptions:
- Death of either party.
- Involuntary conversion (§1033).
- Establishing that neither the exchange nor the disposition had
  tax avoidance as a principal purpose.

§1031(f)(4): related-party rules apply to transfers as part of a
transaction structured to avoid the related-party rule (e.g.,
through a QI). See Teruya Brothers v. Commissioner, 124 T.C. 45
(2005); aff'd 580 F.3d 1038 (9th Cir. 2009).

## Boot computation

Boot = anything received in the exchange that is not like-kind
property:
- Cash received.
- Non-like-kind property received (FMV).
- Net debt relief: debt assumed by counterparty on relinquished
  property MINUS debt assumed by taxpayer on replacement property.

§1031(b): recognized gain = lesser of realized gain OR boot
received. Boot given (e.g., paying down a mortgage at closing) can
offset boot received but cannot create a deductible loss.

Basis of replacement property:
- = Old basis + recognized gain - boot received + boot given
- (Equivalently: substitute basis with fair-market-value step-up
  for boot recognized.)

## Reverse exchange — Rev. Proc. 2000-37

Parking arrangement using an "Exchange Accommodation Titleholder"
(EAT). EAT takes title to either:
- The replacement property (if the taxpayer needs to acquire
  before relinquishing); OR
- The relinquished property.

Safe-harbor requirements (§4 of Rev. Proc. 2000-37):
- Qualified-indicia-of-ownership transferred to EAT.
- Written qualified-exchange-accommodation agreement (QEAA)
  within 5 business days.
- 45-day identification (mirror rule) of relinquished property if
  the EAT holds the replacement.
- 180-day total parking period from transfer to EAT until
  conclusion of exchange.
- EAT bears the burdens and benefits of ownership during parking.
- Taxpayer holds replacement property for productive-use or
  investment purpose.

Rev. Proc. 2003-39 extends similar treatment to multiple-property
reverse exchanges.

## Improvement / Construction exchanges

A variant where the EAT takes title to bare land and the taxpayer
funds construction of improvements before completion of the
exchange. The improvements must be substantially complete before
the EAT transfers title at the end of the parking period; the
taxpayer cannot self-build during the parking period without
voiding the safe harbor.

## Common failure modes

- 45-day deadline missed by 1 day (jurisdictional — no equitable
  cure).
- Constructive receipt during the exchange period (e.g., escrow
  not properly restricted).
- QI is a disqualified person (often the taxpayer's longtime CPA
  or real-estate broker).
- Foreign real property (§1031(h) — flat exclusion).
- Vacation-home in personal use beyond Rev. Proc. 2008-16 limits.
- Personal property exchange post-TCJA (no longer eligible).
- Triangular related-party structures hit by §1031(f)(4).
- Boot mis-characterized; net-debt-relief overlooked.

## State conformity issues

Most states conform to federal §1031, but check per-state file:
- **California** (Cal. Rev. & Tax. Code §18032): post-deferral
  reporting obligation — Form 3840 filed annually until the
  deferred gain is recognized in California.
- **Pennsylvania** historically did not conform for individuals
  (changed for tax years after 12/31/2022).
- Several states have special rules for non-resident sellers.
