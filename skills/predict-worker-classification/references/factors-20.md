# Twenty common-law factors (Rev. Rul. 87-41)

The IRS's twenty-factor test from Rev. Rul. 87-41 has been folded
into the modern three-category framework (behavioral control,
financial control, relationship of the parties), but the twenty
factors remain useful for granular analysis. Reproduced and
annotated here.

## Behavioral control category

1. **Instructions** — the more detailed the instructions to the
   worker about when, where, and how to do the work, the more it
   suggests an employee.
2. **Training** — periodic / ongoing training tilts toward employee.
3. **Integration** — the more the work is integrated into business
   operations, the more it suggests employee.
4. **Services rendered personally** — required personal performance
   suggests employee.
5. **Hiring, supervising, paying assistants** — if the firm hires
   and pays assistants, the worker is more likely an employee.
6. **Continuing relationship** — recurring relationship suggests
   employee.
7. **Set hours of work** — set hours suggest employee.
8. **Full time required** — full-time suggests employee.
9. **Doing work on employer's premises** — work on premises tilts
   toward employee.
10. **Order or sequence set** — required sequence tilts employee.

## Financial control category

11. **Oral or written reports** — required reports tilt employee.
12. **Payment by hour, week, month** — periodic payment suggests
    employee. Payment by job or commission suggests contractor.
13. **Payment of business / traveling expenses** — firm-paid
    expenses tilt employee.
14. **Furnishing tools and materials** — firm-furnished tools tilt
    employee.
15. **Significant investment** — worker investment in own facilities
    suggests contractor.
16. **Realization of profit or loss** — opportunity for both
    suggests contractor.
17. **Working for more than one firm at a time** — multi-firm
    services suggest contractor.
18. **Making service available to general public** — open marketing
    suggests contractor.

## Relationship of the parties category

19. **Right to discharge** — at-will discharge suggests employee.
20. **Right to terminate (worker)** — worker free to quit at any
    time suggests employee. (Originally framed as a control signal
    — the worker who can walk has less skin in the deal than a true
    business operator with a contractual obligation to perform.)

## Modern three-category mapping

Per IRS practitioner-facing guidance:

- **Behavioral control** → factors 1–10 (instructions, training,
  integration, supervision, set hours, premises, sequence).
- **Financial control** → factors 11–18 (reports, payment method,
  expense reimbursement, tools, investment, profit/loss, multi-firm,
  marketing).
- **Relationship of the parties** → factors 19–20 + written contract
  + benefits + permanency + integration.

## Non-determinative factors

No single factor is determinative. Tax courts consistently say the
inquiry is **a totality-of-the-circumstances test**, with the right
to control as the touchstone. See `references/case-law.md` for the
leading cases.

## Industry-specific patterns

- **Trucking**: Owner-operators with their own tractor + paid by
  load + setting their own routes → contractor lean. Company drivers
  paid by mile, in company tractors, with dispatched routes →
  employee lean.
- **Healthcare**: Locum tenens physicians paid by shift via locum
  agencies → contractor lean. Hospital-employed nurses → employee.
- **Construction**: Subcontractors with their own crews and tools →
  contractor lean. Day laborers paid by hour and integrated into a
  GC's daily operations → employee lean.
- **Tech / professional services**: Consultants engaged for a
  defined SOW with their own equipment and multiple clients →
  contractor lean. "Contractors" working full-time for one client
  using client equipment → employee lean.
- **Hairstylists / beauty**: Booth-rental arrangements (worker pays
  rent, sets prices, owns book of business) → contractor lean.
  Commission-based salon employees → employee lean.

## Statutory non-employees (§ 3508)

Even if common-law factors tilt to employee, the Code carves out two
statutory non-employee categories:

1. **Direct sellers** of consumer products in the home, on a buy-
   sell or deposit-commission basis, NOT in a permanent retail
   establishment.
2. **Licensed real-estate agents** whose substantially all
   compensation is directly related to sales output rather than
   hours worked.

Both must have a written contract specifying that the worker is not
to be treated as an employee for federal tax purposes.

## Statutory employees (§ 3121(d)(3))

The reverse: even if common-law factors tilt to contractor, the Code
treats the following as **statutory employees** for FICA only (not
income-tax withholding):

1. Drivers distributing certain products (beverages other than milk,
   meat, vegetables, fruits, bakery products).
2. Full-time life insurance sales agents.
3. Industrial homeworkers.
4. Full-time traveling or city salespersons (as defined).
