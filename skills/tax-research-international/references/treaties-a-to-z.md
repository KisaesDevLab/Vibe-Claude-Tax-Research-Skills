# IRS Treaties A–Z and treaty research workflow

## Source

The IRS publishes a comprehensive list of U.S. tax treaties at:
https://www.irs.gov/businesses/international-businesses/united-states-income-tax-treaties-a-to-z

The Treaties A–Z page links to:
- Full treaty text.
- Treasury Department technical explanations.
- Subsequent protocols and amendments.
- Most-favored-nation clauses (where applicable).

When citing a treaty, use:
- `authority_type: Treaty`
- `weight: primary_statute` (treaties have constitutional treaty
  status equivalent to federal statute under the later-in-time rule
  of §7852(d)(1)).

## Treaty research workflow

### 1. Identify the relevant treaty

For inbound: the treaty between the U.S. and the foreign person's
country of residence.

For outbound: the treaty between the U.S. and the country where
income arises / property is located.

Verify the treaty's effective date and any protocols. Older
treaties (e.g., 1957 USSR treaty for Russia) may still be in force
or may have been replaced.

### 2. Treaty residency

Article 4 of most U.S. treaties defines "resident" with reference
to the foreign country's domestic law and provides a tiebreaker
for dual residents:
1. Permanent home.
2. Center of vital interests.
3. Habitual abode.
4. Nationality.
5. Mutual agreement of competent authorities.

§301.7701(b)-7 — special rules for treaty residence determination
of dual-resident individuals.

### 3. Permanent establishment (PE)

Article 5 of most U.S. treaties defines PE — the threshold for
the source country to tax the foreign person's business profits
under Article 7.

PE includes:
- Place of management.
- Branch.
- Office.
- Factory / workshop.
- Mine / oil-or-gas well / quarry.
- Construction / installation project of more than X months
  (typically 12).
- Dependent agent who habitually exercises authority to conclude
  contracts.

PE typically EXCLUDES:
- Storage / display / delivery of goods.
- Stock of goods maintained solely for processing by another.
- Fixed place of business solely for purchasing goods or
  collecting information.
- Independent agents.

§864(c) ECI rules supplement the PE concept for U.S. taxation of
foreign persons.

### 4. Reduced withholding rates

| Income type | Statutory rate | Typical treaty rate |
|---|---|---|
| Dividends — portfolio | 30% | 15% |
| Dividends — direct (≥ 10%) | 30% | 5% or exempt |
| Interest | 30% | 0–15% (often 0 portfolio interest under §871(h)) |
| Royalties — copyright | 30% | 0–10% |
| Royalties — industrial | 30% | 0–15% |
| Branch profits tax (§884) | 30% | 5–15% or exempt |

Specific rates vary by treaty. Verify the specific Article 10/11/
12 of the treaty.

### 5. Limitation on Benefits (LOB)

Modern U.S. treaties (post-1996 Model, esp. post-2006 and 2016
Models) include detailed LOB provisions to prevent treaty shopping.
LOB tests typically include:
- Publicly traded company.
- Wholly-owned subsidiary of publicly traded company.
- Ownership and base-erosion test.
- Active trade or business test.
- Derivative-benefits test (some treaties only).
- Headquarters test (rare).

Documentation: Form W-8BEN-E for entities; Form 8833 disclosure
for treaty positions inconsistent with the IRC.

### 6. Mutual Agreement Procedure (MAP) and Competent Authority

Article 25 — taxpayers can request U.S. competent-authority
assistance to resolve double-taxation disputes. Rev. Proc. 2015-
40 (and successor) governs MAP request procedures.

MAP is a SLOW process (multi-year). A unilateral filing position
should be considered first, with MAP as a backup.

Note: A U.S.-Competent-Authority resolution is NOT a publicly-
available authority and has limited precedential value.

### 7. §6114 / Form 8833 disclosure

§6114 — when a return position is based on a treaty in a manner
that overrides the IRC, Form 8833 disclosure is required. Failure
triggers §6712 $1,000 per failure ($10,000 per failure for C-corps).

## Common practitioner traps

- Failing to verify treaty effective date / protocols.
- Applying a treaty rate without LOB analysis.
- Treaty residency tiebreaker not invoked for dual residents
  (§301.7701(b)-7).
- §911 FEIE claimed simultaneously with treaty benefits (§911(d)(8)
  — election may waive treaty benefits).
- Form 8833 not filed for treaty-override positions.
- Form W-8BEN-E not collected for foreign payees seeking treaty
  rates.
- Saving clause (Article 1) overlooked: most U.S. treaties retain
  U.S. taxing rights over U.S. citizens regardless of residency.

## Saving clause

Most U.S. treaties contain a "saving clause" that preserves U.S.
taxing rights over U.S. citizens and residents notwithstanding
treaty provisions. Specific exceptions to the saving clause vary
by treaty (e.g., social security, students, teachers).

For U.S. citizens abroad, the treaty typically does NOT exempt
U.S. taxation of foreign-source income. The §901 FTC and §911
FEIE remain the primary tools for double-tax relief.

## Treaty research checklist

- [ ] Confirm treaty in force as of relevant tax year.
- [ ] Confirm protocol amendments and effective dates.
- [ ] Apply Article 4 residency tiebreaker for dual residents.
- [ ] Apply Article 5 PE analysis for inbound business profits.
- [ ] Determine reduced withholding rate per Articles 10/11/12.
- [ ] Apply LOB Article (Article 22 in modern treaties).
- [ ] Form 8833 filed for treaty-override positions.
- [ ] Form W-8BEN / W-8BEN-E collected for foreign payees.
- [ ] Saving-clause analysis for U.S. citizens / residents.
- [ ] Considered MAP / Competent Authority for unresolved
  double-taxation.
