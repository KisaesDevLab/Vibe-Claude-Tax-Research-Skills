# CFC, GILTI, Subpart F, and PFIC mechanics

## Controlled Foreign Corporation (CFC)

§957(a) — foreign corporation in which more than 50% (vote or
value) is owned by U.S. shareholders.
§951(b) — U.S. shareholder is a U.S. person who owns 10% or more
(vote or value) of the foreign corporation.

Constructive ownership rules at §958(a) (direct/indirect) and
§958(b) (constructive — modified §318).

TCJA repealed §958(b)(4), making "downward attribution" possible
between foreign parent and U.S. subsidiary. Notice 2018-13 and
subsequent guidance address the consequences.

## Subpart F income (§§951–965)

§951(a) — U.S. shareholder of CFC includes pro-rata share of
Subpart F income.

§952 categories of Subpart F income:
- §953 — insurance income.
- §954(c) — foreign personal-holding-company income (FPHCI):
  passive interest, dividends, royalties, rents, gains, etc.
- §954(d) — foreign-base-company sales income.
- §954(e) — foreign-base-company services income.
- §954(b)(3) — de-minimis, full-inclusion, and high-tax exception.

§954(b)(4) — high-tax exception: Subpart F income excluded if
foreign tax rate exceeds 90% of U.S. corporate rate (currently
18.9%). Election made annually.

§952(c) — current-year and accumulated E&P limit on Subpart F
inclusion.

## GILTI (§951A)

§951A — global intangible low-taxed income inclusion.

Formula:
1. Net CFC tested income = sum of tested income/loss across CFCs.
2. Net deemed tangible-income return = 10% × Qualified Business
   Asset Investment (QBAI) − certain interest expense.
3. GILTI = Net CFC tested income − Net deemed tangible-income
   return.

§250(a) — 50% deduction (37.5% post-2025 absent extension) on
GILTI inclusion. Section 250 deduction subject to taxable-income
limitation.

§960(d) — indirect FTC at 80% of foreign taxes attributable to
tested income.

§954(b)(4) high-tax exception — proposed regulations (2019)
extended high-tax exception to GILTI; finalized at §1.951A-2(c)(7)
in 2020 with election.

## §245A — territorial DRD

100% DRD on §245A-eligible foreign-source dividend received from
specified 10%-owned foreign corporation (≥ 10% and not a CFC of
which the U.S. shareholder is a §951 inclusion shareholder).

§245A(e) — hybrid-dividend exception (no DRD if dividend gives
rise to deduction in foreign country).

§1.245A regs (2019, 2020) restrict DRD on certain "extraordinary
dispositions" and "extraordinary reductions" — anti-abuse rules.

## §59A BEAT

Modified §59A — base erosion and anti-abuse tax. Imposes minimum
tax of 10% (post-2025: 12.5% — verify post-OBBBA status) on
"modified taxable income" of large multinationals (≥ $500M average
gross receipts for 3 prior years AND base-erosion percentage ≥ 3%).

Modified taxable income = taxable income + base erosion tax
benefits (deductions for related-party payments to foreign
parties).

## §250 FDII

Foreign-Derived Intangible Income deduction: U.S. corporations
exporting goods or services derive a 37.5% deduction on FDII
(reduced to 21.875% post-2025 absent extension — verify post-
OBBBA status).

FDII formula similar to GILTI but inverted: FDII = deemed-
intangible-income × (foreign-derived deduction-eligible income /
deduction-eligible income).

## PFIC (§§1291–1298)

§1297 — foreign corporation is a PFIC if:
- §1297(a)(1) — 75% or more of gross income is passive (Income
  Test); OR
- §1297(a)(2) — 50% or more of assets produce passive income
  (Asset Test).

Three regimes for U.S. shareholders:

1. **§1291 default**: excess-distribution / disposition treated
   as ordinary income with interest charge. Punitive.

2. **§1295 QEF (Qualified Electing Fund) election**: shareholder
   includes pro-rata share of PFIC's earnings annually; gain on
   disposition is capital. Need annual PFIC information statement.

3. **§1296 mark-to-market election**: available for marketable
   PFIC stock. Mark to FMV annually; ordinary income on gain,
   ordinary loss on loss (limited to prior MTM gains).

§1298(f) — Form 8621 reporting required for ALL U.S. shareholders
of PFICs annually.

## §367 — outbound and inbound transactions

§367(a) — outbound transfers of property to foreign corporations:
gain recognition unless §367(a)(3) active-trade-or-business
exception (limited post-TCJA — §367(a)(3)(C) repealed by TCJA).

§367(b) — inbound and foreign-to-foreign reorganizations: gain or
income recognition to ensure subpart-F-type inclusions.

§367(d) — transfers of intangible property to foreign corporations:
deemed §351 transfer with annual royalty inclusion.

§1.367(a) and §1.367(b) regs are extensive and country-specific.

§5314 / §5321 FBAR penalties: $10,000 non-willful per violation
(per Bittner, per FBAR not per account); willful penalty up to
greater of $100,000 or 50% of account balance.

## Common practitioner traps

- §951(b) 10% threshold checks at each U.S.-person level (not
  combined within a controlled group for the threshold).
- §958(b)(4) downward-attribution post-TCJA creates unexpected
  CFCs from a U.S. perspective when a foreign parent owns U.S. and
  foreign subsidiaries.
- GILTI high-tax election made for all CFCs or none (consistency
  rule).
- PFIC default §1291 regime triggers unwelcome ordinary-income +
  interest charge for missed QEF elections; consider §1298(b)(1)
  retroactive QEF where eligible.
- §367(a)(3)(C) TCJA repeal of active-trade exception for
  outbound goodwill transfers triggered substantial recognition.
- Form 5471 categories (1–5) drive different schedules; failure
  to identify the right category triggers the $10K penalty.
- FBAR civil penalty separate from §6038D Form 8938 penalty —
  different agencies (FinCEN vs IRS) and different exhaustion
  paths.
