# 10 Tax Strategies for Schedule C Taxpayers — Schedule C / SE Map

> **Bottom line:** Sole proprietorships (and single-member LLCs
> taxed as sole props) have **structural advantages** that S corp
> owners don't, particularly: (1) hire-your-child FICA/FUTA
> exemptions under §3121(b)(3)(A) / §3306(c)(5); (2) §105 HRA
> spouse-employee strategy that survived ACA's market reforms only
> in the single-employee context (Notice 2015-17); and (3) Schedule
> E rental income avoids SE tax under §1402(a)(1). The **trade-off
> against S corp treatment** is full SE tax on net earnings, no
> reasonable-comp arbitrage, and missing access to certain fringe
> benefits.

This catalog maps **10 strategies** to specific lines on Schedule C
(2025 form) and Form 8829, with authorities, OBBBA impact, and
cross-references to detailed treatment in the master Bradford
strategies pack.

## D1. §105 HRA Spouse-Employee Health Plan

**Schedule C line:** 14 (Employee benefit programs).

**Authority:**

- IRC §105(b) (employer reimbursement of medical expenses for
  employee, spouse, dependents excluded from employee's income)
- IRC §162(a) (employer deduction for employee benefits)
- Rev. Rul. 71-588 (sole prop can employ spouse; family medical
  reimbursement valid)
- PLR 9409006 (confirmed Rev. Rul. 71-588 in spouse-employee
  context)
- Notice 2013-54 (ACA market reforms — stand-alone HRAs prohibited
  for 2+ employees)
- Notice 2015-17 (single-employee HRA transition relief —
  permanent for one-employee employers)
- 21st Century Cures Act §18001 (QSEHRA — added 2016 for
  small-employer reimbursement)
- T.D. 9867 (ICHRA regulations effective 2020)

**Strategy.** Sole prop hires spouse as bona fide W-2 employee;
establishes §105 HRA covering family medical expenses; spouse's
family coverage includes the proprietor (as employee's spouse).
Result: family medical expenses become fully deductible business
expenses on Schedule C.

**Critical eligibility:** Must have **only one employee** (the
spouse). Adding any non-spouse employee triggers ACA market reform
problems. Migration path is QSEHRA (≤50 FTEs) or ICHRA (any size).

**Mechanics:**

1. Hire spouse formally — W-4, I-9, payroll setup.
2. Establish written §105 plan defining eligible medical expenses
   (per §213(d)), family coverage scope, dependents.
3. Spouse incurs medical expenses for self, the proprietor (as
   spouse), and dependents.
4. Spouse submits expense reports with §213(d) substantiation
   (receipts, EOBs, prescription records).
5. Schedule C deducts as employee benefits (Line 14).
6. Spouse excludes from income under §105(b).

**OBBBA impact:** No direct OBBBA changes to §105. Strategy
preserved.

**Pitfalls:**

- **Multi-employee disqualification** triggers ACA reform problems.
- **Bona fide employment required** — IRS scrutinizes; reasonable
  wages, time records, real work.
- **PCORI fee** (~$3.47/covered life for 2025) due annually via
  Form 720.
- **State conformity** — health-plan regulation varies by state.
- Sole prop preserves single-employee rule; cannot use this in
  S corp (§1372 partner-treatment).

**Cross-reference:** See master Bradford pack Skill D1 for full
authority chain and mechanics.

## D2. Employ Your Child (Sole Prop / Parent-Only Partnership)

**Schedule C lines:** 26 (Wages), 23 (Taxes & Licenses — for any
state SUI / federal income tax withholding tracking).

**Authority:**

- IRC §162(a) (deductible wages)
- IRC §3121(b)(3)(A) (FICA exemption for child < 18 in
  parent's sole prop or parent-only partnership)
- IRC §3306(c)(5) (FUTA exemption for child < 21)
- Pub. L. 119-21 (OBBBA — standard deduction $15,750 (2025) /
  $16,100 (2026))
- IRC §152 (definition of child)
- Eller v. Commissioner, T.C. Memo. 1981-322 (substance over
  form for child employment)
- Denman v. Commissioner, T.C. Memo. 2000-220 (child not bona
  fide employee — wages disallowed)

**Strategy.** Sole prop employs child to perform legitimate,
age-appropriate business work. Combined effects:

- **Income tax shifting:** Wages deducted at parent's marginal
  rate (potentially 24-37% federal); child uses own standard
  deduction ($15,750 for 2025) — wages within standard deduction
  produce $0 tax to child.
- **FICA exemption:** Wages to child < 18 in sole prop / parent-
  only partnership are FICA-exempt (15.3% combined savings vs.
  S corp where FICA applies).
- **FUTA exemption:** Child < 21 in sole prop / parent-only
  partnership — FUTA exempt.
- **Roth IRA opportunity:** Child with earned income can
  contribute to Roth IRA up to lesser of $7,000 (2025) or earned
  income.

**Critical: Entity structure determines exemption.**

| Employer Entity | FICA < 18 | FUTA < 21 |
|-----------------|-----------|-----------|
| Sole proprietorship | EXEMPT | EXEMPT |
| Single-member LLC (default) | EXEMPT | EXEMPT |
| Partnership where ALL partners are parent | EXEMPT | EXEMPT |
| Partnership with non-parent partner | NOT exempt | NOT exempt |
| S corporation | NOT exempt | NOT exempt |
| C corporation | NOT exempt | NOT exempt |
| LLC taxed as S/C corp | NOT exempt | NOT exempt |

**Pitfalls:**

- **Substance over form** — IRS audits hire-your-child for real
  work performed. Document job description, time records, age-
  appropriate tasks, reasonable wage.
- **State child labor laws** — federal OSHA + state restrictions
  on hours, types of work, school attendance.
- **Reasonable wage** — court-tested floor: ~$5-15/hour for
  age-appropriate clerical work. $50/hour to stuff envelopes
  fails.
- **Documentation: W-4, I-9, time records, payroll register, W-2.**
- **Don't classify as 1099 contractor** — eliminates FICA exemption
  and triggers SE tax for the child.
- **Roth IRA contribution** must be funded by earned income (not
  parent gift); transfer wages to child's own bank account.

**Cross-reference:** Strategy Library #49 for comprehensive
hire-your-child treatment with state-by-state child labor coordination
and payroll mechanics.

## D3. Employ Your Spouse (Schedule C)

**Schedule C lines:** 26 (Wages), 14 (Employee benefits via §105
HRA — see D1), 23 (Taxes — payroll taxes).

**Authority:**

- IRC §162(a)
- IRC §105(b) (cross-ref D1)
- Rev. Rul. 71-588
- PLR 9409006

**Strategy.** Sole prop employs spouse to provide legitimate
services. Two parallel benefits:

1. **§105 HRA family medical** (primary benefit — see D1).
2. **Reasonable wages** deductible on Schedule C.

**FICA tradeoff:** Unlike children under 18, **spouse wages ARE
subject to FICA** (15.3% combined). Compare to:

- Schedule C net earnings: 15.3% SE tax (with 7.65% above-the-line
  deduction for half).
- Spouse W-2 wages: 7.65% employer share + 7.65% employee share =
  15.3% combined; deduction reduces SE tax base by same amount.

**Optimization:** Pay spouse **minimum reasonable wage** to qualify
as bona fide employee (typically $5,000-$15,000/year), then
maximize §105 HRA reimbursements (no FICA on medical
reimbursements).

**Pitfalls:**

- **Bona fide employment** required (substance over form).
- **FICA on wages** — unlike child.
- **Joint vs. separate accounts** — if cash flows fully commingled,
  difficult to substantiate employment.
- **Spouse becomes wage employee** — affects eligibility for
  certain programs (e.g., Medicaid, SSI) and affects SS earnings
  history.

**Cross-reference:** Skill D1 for §105 HRA mechanics.

## D4. Rent from Your Spouse — Schedule E SE Tax Avoidance

**Schedule C line:** 20a or 20b (Rent or lease — vehicles/equipment
or other business property).

**Authority:**

- IRC §162(a)
- IRC §1402(a)(1) (SE tax exclusion for rentals from real estate
  and personal property leased with real estate)
- IRC §469 (passive activity rules; self-rental rules under
  Reg. §1.469-2(f)(6))
- Bobo v. Commissioner, T.C. Memo. 1989-329 (substantial-services
  standard for trade-or-business vs. rental real estate)
- CCA 202151005 (Dec 2021 — vacation rental substantial-services
  analysis)
- Reg. §1.1402(a)-4 (rental real estate exclusion mechanics)

**Strategy.** Sole prop rents office space, equipment, vehicles, or
real property **from spouse** at FMV. Rental income reported on
**Schedule E** by spouse (or as personal property rental income on
Form 1040 Schedule 1). Schedule E rental income is NOT subject to
SE tax. The proprietor deducts the rent on Schedule C (subject to
SE tax base reduction).

**Net effect:** Income shifted from SE-taxed Schedule C to
non-SE-taxed Schedule E.

**Critical: Bobo standard.**

If spouse provides "**services usually or customarily provided**"
(housekeeping for hotels, daily linens, meals, on-call concierge),
income recharacterized as Schedule C trade or business →
SE-taxable. Per Bobo and CCA 202151005:

| Service Level | Schedule | SE Tax |
|---------------|----------|--------|
| Pure rental (utilities, repairs) | E | No |
| Substantial services (hotel-like) | C | Yes |

**§469 self-rental rules:** Treas. Reg. §1.469-2(f)(6) recharacterizes
"net rental income" from self-rented property to "non-passive"
when leased to a trade or business in which the taxpayer materially
participates. **Spouse-to-spouse rental** — each spouse is a
separate taxpayer for §469 purposes per Reg. §1.469-1T(j), so
self-rental rules potentially apply but coordination with §1402
(SE) operates independently.

**Mechanics:**

1. Spouse owns the asset (real estate, equipment) in own name.
2. Written lease at FMV (3 comparable rate quotes).
3. Sole prop pays rent — separate transaction, business → personal/
   rental account.
4. Spouse reports rent on **Schedule E** (real property) or
   personal property rental income (Form 1040 Schedule 1, Line 24b
   — "Income from rental of personal property…").
5. Sole prop deducts on Schedule C Line 20a (vehicles/equipment) or
   20b (other business property).

**Pitfalls:**

- **§469 PAL limits** at spouse's level (if rental shows loss).
- **Bobo / substantial services** trap.
- **§280A vacation home rules** if spouse uses property personally.
- **FMV substantiation** — without market rate documentation, IRS
  may recharacterize.
- **Self-rental rules under §469** — verify spouse-to-spouse
  treatment carefully.
- **Personal property only (no real estate)** — non-real-estate
  rentals are SE-taxable under §1402(a)(1) UNLESS leased "with"
  real estate. Equipment-only rentals to spouse may be SE-taxable
  to the spouse.

## D5. Home Office Expenses (Schedule C)

**Schedule C line:** 30 (Expenses for business use of home).
**Form:** Form 8829 (actual expense method) OR simplified method.

**Authority:**

- IRC §280A
- IRC §280A(c)(1) (exclusive/regular use; principal place of
  business / clients meet / separate structure)
- Reg. §1.280A
- Commissioner v. Soliman, 506 U.S. 168 (1993) (relative
  importance + time test for principal place of business)
- Taxpayer Relief Act of 1997 §932 — overrode Soliman effective
  for tax years beginning after Dec. 31, 1998: home office
  qualifies as principal place of business if (1) used
  exclusively and regularly for **administrative or management**
  activities AND (2) no other fixed location for substantial
  administrative activities.
- Pub. 587 (Business Use of Your Home)

**Strategy.** Sole prop deducts home office expenses **directly**
on Form 8829 (no accountable plan needed because there's no
employer-employee distinction).

**Two methods:**

| Feature | Actual Expense (Form 8829) | Simplified Method |
|---------|--------------------------|------------------|
| Calculation | % × actual expenses | $5 × sq ft (max 300 sq ft = $1,500) |
| Form | Form 8829 | Schedule C Line 30 only |
| Depreciation | Yes (39-year SL on residential) | None |
| Carryforward | Yes (excess deductions) | None |
| §1250 unrecaptured gain on sale | Yes (max 25% per §1(h)(6)(A)) | No |
| Maximum deduction | Unlimited (subject to §280A net income limit) | $1,500/year |

**Critical post-1999 rule:** Having an outside office does NOT
disqualify the home office, provided:

1. Home office used exclusively/regularly for **administrative or
   management** activities.
2. No other fixed location used for substantial administrative
   work.

This benefits taxpayers who provide services at customer locations
(real estate agents, electricians, plumbers, photographers, sales
reps, contractors).

**Synergy with vehicle deduction (Skill D7):** When home office
qualifies as principal place of business, **trips from home to
customer/client locations become business miles** rather than
non-deductible commuting.

**Pitfalls:**

- **Exclusive use violation** — kids' homework area, personal
  computer use, dual-use room.
- **Regular use violation** — sporadic or rare use.
- **§121 home sale recapture** — depreciation under actual expense
  method recaptured as unrecaptured §1250 gain (max 25% per
  §1(h)(6)(A)).
- **§280A(c)(5) net income limitation** — home office deductions
  cannot exceed gross income from home office activity (with
  carryover).
- **Simplified method** has no §1250 recapture but caps at $1,500.
- **State conformity varies** (NY, NJ, CA particularly).
- **Pre-1999 case law (Soliman)** still relevant for cases that
  don't meet the administrative/management test.

**Cross-reference:** Skill A (master pack) for S corp version
(very different — uses accountable plan reimbursement).

## D6. De Minimis Fringe Benefits

**Schedule C line:** 14 (Employee benefit programs) — for benefits
provided to employees. For sole prop's own benefits, included in
ordinary expenses (Line 27a or specific line based on category).

**Authority:**

- IRC §132(e)
- Reg. §1.132-6
- Notice 2011-72 (cell phone integration)

**Strategy.** Sole prop provides employees (spouse, child,
non-family staff) with **occasional, low-value gifts and benefits**
that are excluded from the employee's income AND deductible to the
business under §132(e):

- Occasional flowers, fruit, books on special occasions.
- Coffee, donuts, soft drinks at workplace.
- Holiday gifts of property (NOT cash or cash equivalents).
- Occasional theater or sporting event tickets.
- Group meals (occasional company picnic, holiday party).

**Authority detail:**

Reg. §1.132-6(b)(2): Frequency test — value must be infrequent
enough that accounting for it is administratively impracticable.

Reg. §1.132-6(c): **Cash and cash equivalents NEVER qualify as de
minimis.** Even a $5 gift card to Starbucks fails. Gift cards to
specific retailers may be cash equivalents depending on form.

**Customary thresholds (not statutory):**

- IRS examiners generally accept individual items up to ~$75-$100.
- Frequent (monthly/biweekly) gifts FAIL — they're regular
  compensation.

**Pitfalls:**

- Cash & cash equivalents fail.
- Frequent gifts fail.
- **§274 entertainment limits** apply separately to event tickets
  for the BUSINESS deduction (the de minimis exclusion only
  addresses the EMPLOYEE income side).
- **Holiday party (§274(e)(4))** — fully deductible (NOT subject
  to 50% cut applicable to other employee meals).
- Documentation should describe the occasion and business purpose
  briefly (e.g., "thank-you bouquet for client retention").

## D7. Vehicle Expenses — Heavy Vehicle + Home Office Combo

**Schedule C line:** 9 (Car and truck expenses).

**Authority:**

- IRC §179
- IRC §168(k) — 100% bonus depreciation post-1/19/2025 (OBBBA)
- IRC §280F (luxury auto cap exemption for vehicles >6,000 lb GVWR)
- IRC §280A(c)(1) (home office principal-place-of-business test)
- Reg. §1.162-2(a) (commuting vs. business mileage)
- Pub. 463
- Notice 2026-11 (post-OBBBA bonus depreciation interim guidance)
- Strategy Library #4 (Heavy Vehicle Strategy)

**Strategy.** Combined synergistic strategy:

**Component 1 — Heavy vehicle (>6,000 lb GVWR).** Vehicles with
GVWR over 6,000 lbs are NOT subject to §280F luxury auto caps.
Combined with §179 ($31,300 SUV cap for 2025) and 100% bonus
depreciation post-OBBBA, large portions of vehicle cost are
deducted in Year 1.

**Component 2 — Home office as principal place of business.** When
home office qualifies under post-1999 §280A(c)(1), trips from home
to customer/client locations are **business miles** (deductible)
rather than **commuting miles** (non-deductible). Higher business-
use percentage on the vehicle increases the deductible portion of
all vehicle expenses.

**OBBBA Example (2026 acquisition):**

Sole prop electrician (with qualifying home office) buys $65,000
used Ford F-250 (GVWR 10,000 lbs) on Feb 15, 2026. Total miles
25,000; business miles 20,000 = 80% business use.

| Component | Amount |
|-----------|--------|
| Business basis (80% × $65,000) | $52,000 |
| §179 expensing (SUV cap) | $31,300 |
| 100% bonus on remainder | $20,700 |
| **Year 1 deduction** | **$52,000** |

**Documentation required:**

- Mileage log per §274(d) (no Cohan estimation).
- Vehicle purchase receipt.
- Home office substantiation per §280A(c)(1).
- Business purpose for each trip.

**Pitfalls:**

- **Standard mileage method blocks §179/bonus** — once standard
  mileage is used, switching to actual expense limits depreciation
  to straight-line.
- **Business use must exceed 50%** in placed-in-service year for
  accelerated methods.
- **Recapture if business use drops** below 50% during recovery
  period.
- **Mixed personal/business trips** — mileage log must
  distinguish.
- **Home office disqualification** drops business mileage %
  significantly.

**Cross-reference:** Strategy Library #4 (Heavy Vehicle); Skill B
master pack (S corp accountable plan version with PLR 200930029).

## D8. Domestic Travel — Majority of Days Rule

**Schedule C line:** 24a (Travel) — 100% transportation; lodging
and meals at 50% under §274(n).

**Authority:**

- IRC §162(a)(2) (travel away from home)
- IRC §274(d) (strict substantiation — Cohan unavailable)
- IRC §274(n) (50% meal limit)
- Reg. §1.162-2(b) (allocation rules for business/personal trips)
- Pub. 463

**Strategy.** For domestic travel:

- If **majority of trip days are business days**: 100% of
  direct-route transportation deductible.
- Lodging and meals deductible only for business days (meals at
  50%).

**Business day categories** (per Reg. §1.162-2 and Pub. 463):

1. **Substantially business** — 5+ business hours.
2. **Standby days** — waiting for client meetings.
3. **Travel days** — going to and returning from business
   destination.
4. **Sandwich rule** — weekends sandwiched between business days
   count as business.
5. **Saturday-night-stayover** — counts as business if produces
   travel cost savings (cheaper airfare).

**Pitfalls:**

- **§274(d) absolute substantiation** — daily journal of activities
  required; no Cohan estimation.
- **Lavish or extravagant** under §162(a)(2).
- **Spouse travel** — generally non-deductible unless spouse is
  bona fide employee.
- **Per diem alternative** — Rev. Proc. 2019-48 (or current)
  permits per diem method; simplifies recordkeeping but requires
  consistency.

**Cross-reference:** Skill C9 (S corp travel reimbursement) and
Skill D9 (foreign travel under §274(c)).

## D9. Foreign Travel — §274(c) Seven-Day / 25% Rule

**Schedule C line:** 24a (Travel).

**Authority:**

- IRC §274(c)(1) — general rule (primarily related to trade or
  business)
- IRC §274(c)(2)(A) — **7-day exception** (trip ≤ 1 week, 100%
  transportation)
- IRC §274(c)(2)(B) — **25% exception** (personal time < 25% of
  total travel time)
- IRC §274(d) — strict substantiation
- IRC §274(h) — convention restrictions outside North American area
- Reg. §1.274-4 — examples and computation rules
- Pub. 463

**Strategy.** Foreign travel has **two special exceptions** that
allow full transportation deductibility without the domestic
"majority of days" test:

**Exception 1: 7-Day Rule (§274(c)(2)(A)).** If foreign trip is
**less than one week** (counting day of return but NOT day of
departure), 100% of transportation is deductible regardless of
personal time.

**Exception 2: 25% Rule (§274(c)(2)(B)).** If personal-use time
is **less than 25%** of total travel time, 100% of transportation
is deductible.

If **neither** exception applies, transportation is allocated:
business days / total days.

**Examples:**

| Scenario | Trip Length | Business Days | Personal Days | Test | Result |
|----------|-------------|---------------|----------------|------|--------|
| 1 | 6 days London | 4 | 2 | 7-Day Rule | 100% deductible |
| 2 | 14 days Tokyo | 11 | 3 | 25% Rule (3/14 = 21%) | 100% deductible |
| 3 | 10 days Paris | 5 | 5 | Neither | 50% allocated |

**Counting days (Reg. §1.274-4):**

- Day of return COUNTS.
- Day of departure does NOT count.
- Travel days (US to foreign country) typically count as business.
- Sandwich rule applies (weekends between business days).

**Pitfalls:**

- **§274(d) absolute substantiation** — daily records.
- **Vacation immediately before/after business** counts as
  personal.
- **Spouse / family travel** generally non-deductible.
- **Conventions abroad (§274(h))** — additional restriction:
  taxpayer must show "as reasonable for meeting outside the
  North American area as within."

**Cross-reference:** Skill D8 (domestic travel); Skill C9 (S corp
travel reimbursement).

## D10. Cell Phone Expenses (Schedule C)

**Schedule C lines:** 25 (Utilities) for monthly bills; Line 13
(Depreciation/§179) or Line 22 (Supplies) for device purchase under
$2,500 (de minimis safe harbor under Reg. §1.263(a)-1(f)).

**Authority:**

- IRC §132(d) (working condition fringe — for employees)
- IRC §132(e) (de minimis fringe — for employees)
- IRC §162(a) (ordinary business expense — for sole prop's own
  phone)
- Notice 2011-72 (post-2010 cell phone treatment)
- Small Business Jobs Act of 2010, §2043 (removed cell phones
  from §280F listed property)
- Reg. §1.263(a)-1(f) ($2,500 de minimis safe harbor for tangible
  property)

**Strategy (for proprietor's own phone).** The sole proprietor IS
the business — there's no employer-employee distinction. The
proprietor deducts:

1. **Business use percentage** of monthly cell phone bill on
   Schedule C Line 25 (Utilities).
2. **Device purchase**:
   - Under $2,500: Expense via §1.263(a)-1(f) de minimis safe
     harbor on Line 22 (Supplies) or Line 27a (Other expenses).
   - $2,500+: Depreciate or §179 expense on Form 4562 (Line 13
     of Schedule C reflects total).

**Strategy (for employees — spouse, child, staff).** Apply Notice
2011-72:

- Cell phone provided "primarily for noncompensatory business
  reasons" → **Working condition fringe under §132(d)** for
  business use; **de minimis fringe under §132(e)** for personal
  use; **NO substantiation of personal use required.**
- Schedule C deducts full cost on Line 14 (Employee benefits) or
  Line 25 (Utilities).
- Employee excludes from W-2 wages.

**Notice 2011-72 noncompensatory business reasons** (examples):

- Need to contact employee at all times for work-related
  emergencies.
- Need to be available to speak with clients when away from
  office.
- Need to speak with clients in different time zones outside
  normal business hours.

**Notice 2011-72 EXPLICITLY EXCLUDES** as noncompensatory:

- Phones provided to promote employee morale.
- Phones provided to attract a prospective employee.
- Phones provided as substitute for additional compensation.

**Pitfalls:**

- **Cash phone allowance fails** — flat $X/month allowance without
  substantiation is taxable wages.
- **Compensatory primary purpose** — if phone is substitute for
  additional comp, Notice 2011-72 doesn't apply.
- **Multiple personal phones** — single business-purpose phone
  contemplated; multiple raises scrutiny.
- **Listed property (pre-2010) treatment** — for tax years before
  2010, cell phones required §274(d) substantiation. Post-2010,
  Small Business Jobs Act §2043 removed from listed property.

**OBBBA impact:** Notice 2011-72 unchanged. §179 / 100% bonus
depreciation available for device purchase cost (subject to §179
totals).

**Cross-reference:** Skill C10 (S corp version — same Notice
2011-72 mechanics with accountable plan).

## Implementation — Schedule C Workflow Integration

### Annual Workflow Triggers

For each Schedule C engagement, prompt practitioner with:

1. **D1 / D3 — Spouse strategies.** Ask:
   - Is taxpayer married?
   - Does spouse work elsewhere (if so, employer plan may cover
     family)?
   - Family medical expenses anticipated?
   → If yes to married + no spouse coverage + significant medical:
     **§105 HRA spouse-employee opportunity.**

2. **D2 — Child employment.** Ask:
   - Does taxpayer have children?
   - Ages? (FICA exemption < 18; FUTA exemption < 21)
   - Legitimate work child can perform?
   → If yes: **Hire-your-child opportunity.**

3. **D4 — Spouse rental.** Ask:
   - Does spouse own real estate, equipment, vehicles used in
     business?
   → If yes: **Rent-from-spouse opportunity.**

4. **D5 — Home office.** Ask:
   - Does taxpayer use home for business?
   - Other office locations?
   → If yes home, no other admin office: **Principal-place-of-
     business under post-1999 §280A(c)(1).**

5. **D7 — Heavy vehicle + home office combo.** Ask:
   - Vehicle GVWR > 6,000 lbs?
   - Home office qualifies as principal place of business?
   → If yes: **Combo strategy with OBBBA 100% bonus.**

6. **D8 / D9 — Travel.** Ask:
   - Domestic or foreign?
   - Days breakdown (business vs. personal)?
   → Apply §1.162-2(b) majority-of-days (domestic) or §274(c)
     7-day/25% (foreign).

### State Conformity Review

States with significant non-conformity to federal rules:

- **California (CA)** — does not conform to OBBBA bonus
  depreciation; ADS depreciation. Home office rules same as
  federal. §105 HRA conforms but ACA reform interaction varies.
- **New York (NY)** — Form IT-225 modifications for federal
  bonus depreciation. Home office stricter than federal in some
  cases.
- **Massachusetts (MA)** — does not conform to bonus depreciation;
  separate state rules.
- **Hawaii, Minnesota** — partial conformity to bonus.
- **Texas, Florida, Nevada, S. Dakota, Wash., Wyo., Tenn.** —
  no state income tax (non-conformity moot).
