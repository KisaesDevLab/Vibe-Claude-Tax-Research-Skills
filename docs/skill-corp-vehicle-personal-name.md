# Corporate Vehicle in Personal Name — Accountable Plan Reimbursement

> **Bottom line:** When a vehicle used for corporate business is
> titled in the owner-employee's **personal name** (rather than
> the corporation's), the only effective way to capture the full
> business-use deduction — including §179 expensing, §168(k)
> bonus depreciation, and operating expenses — is **accountable-
> plan reimbursement** to the employee under Reg. §1.62-2.
> Claiming the deduction on the personal Form 1040 (via Form
> 2106 / Schedule A unreimbursed employee expenses) loses some
> or all of the deduction to the **§67(g) suspension of
> miscellaneous itemized deductions** for tax years 2018-2025,
> and even after sunset is hobbled by AMT and 2%-of-AGI floor
> issues.

## Why title-in-personal-name happens

Common reasons a corporate-business vehicle is titled in the
owner's personal name rather than the corporation's:

- **New corporation.** Vehicle was purchased before the
  corporation was formed.
- **Financing terms.** Personal financing rate is better than
  commercial.
- **Insurance rates.** Personal-auto policy often cheaper than
  commercial.
- **Liability exposure.** Owner prefers personal-titled to
  isolate from corporation creditors.
- **State law/registration.** Some state DMV regimes make
  personal titling more practical.

None of these change the federal income tax result if the
accountable-plan reimbursement method is properly executed. Per
**PLR 200930029** (extensively cited in practitioner literature),
the IRS confirmed that **§179 does NOT require corporate title**
to qualify for the deduction — the corporation may deduct §179
and depreciation on a vehicle titled in the owner's personal name
when the vehicle is used predominantly in the corporation's trade
or business and the corporation reimburses the owner under an
accountable plan.

## Why the alternative (Form 2106 / Schedule A) fails

For tax years **2018-2025**, **§67(g)** (added by TCJA) suspends
ALL miscellaneous itemized deductions subject to the 2%-of-AGI
floor. Form 2106 unreimbursed employee business expenses are in
this category. Effect: a corporate owner-employee who takes
business-vehicle deductions on their personal return gets **$0
deduction**, regardless of magnitude.

Even when §67(g) sunsets after 2025 (assuming no extension —
verify under OBBBA `[CITATION NEEDED — search: OBBBA §67(g) Pub.
L. 119-21]`), three structural problems remain:

1. **Itemize-or-lose.** Standard deduction-takers get nothing.
2. **2%-of-AGI floor.** Reduces deduction by 2% of AGI.
3. **AMT add-back.** Miscellaneous itemized deductions are AMT
   preferences — taxpayers in AMT lose them entirely.

## The §179 expensing landscape (2025/2026)

Under the **One Big Beautiful Bill Act of 2025 (OBBBA, Pub. L.
119-21, signed July 4, 2025)**, the §179 expensing limits for
2025 and beyond are dramatically more favorable than under prior
law:

| Provision | 2024 Pre-OBBBA | 2025 Post-OBBBA | 2026 |
|---|---|---|---|
| §179 deduction limit | $1,160,000 | **$2,500,000** | $2,560,000 (inflation-adjusted) |
| §179 phase-out threshold | $2,890,000 | **$4,000,000** | $4,089,000 (inflation-adjusted) |
| Complete phase-out | $4,050,000 | $6,500,000 | ~$6,649,000 |
| §179(b)(5) SUV cap | $30,500 | **$31,300** | $32,000 |
| §168(k) bonus depreciation | 60% | **100%** (after 1/19/2025) | 100% (permanent) |

**OBBBA §168(k) bonus depreciation:** Permanently restored to
**100%** for qualified property **acquired after January 19,
2025**. Property acquired on or before January 19, 2025 falls
under prior-law phase-down (40% for 2025).

**Authority for current limits:**

- **§179(b)(1)** — annual deduction limit (post-OBBBA: $2.5M for
  tax years beginning in 2025).
- **§179(b)(2)** — phase-out threshold (post-OBBBA: $4M).
- **§179(b)(5)** — SUV cap (passenger autos with GVWR 6,001-
  14,000 lbs, primarily designed to carry passengers): inflation-
  adjusted; **$31,300** for 2025 (Rev. Proc. 2024-40); **$32,000**
  for 2026 (Rev. Proc. 2025-32).
- **§168(k)(1) and (k)(8)** as amended by OBBBA — 100% bonus for
  qualified property acquired after 1/19/2025.
- **Notice 2026-11** — interim post-OBBBA guidance on §168(k);
  preserves familiar Reg. §1.168(k)-2 framework with new dates.

## Vehicle classification for Section 179 purposes

The **Gross Vehicle Weight Rating (GVWR)** — found on the
manufacturer's certification label on the driver's-side door
jamb — determines tax treatment.

### Light passenger automobiles (GVWR ≤ 6,000 lbs)

Subject to **§280F luxury auto caps** (annual depreciation
limits set by IRS revenue procedure). Applies regardless of
business use percentage.

For 2025 passenger automobiles (Rev. Proc. 2025-X — verify
current):

- **Year 1:** ~$20,400 (with bonus depreciation)
- **Year 2:** ~$19,600
- **Year 3:** ~$11,800
- **Subsequent years:** ~$7,060

Without bonus, year 1 cap is approximately $12,200.

### Heavy SUVs (GVWR 6,001-14,000 lbs, primarily passenger)

Subject to **§179(b)(5) SUV cap** of **$31,300 for 2025** ($32,000
for 2026). Remaining basis can take §168(k) bonus depreciation
(no SUV cap on bonus). NOT subject to §280F passenger-auto caps.

**Combined Year-1 strategy:**
- §179 expense up to $31,300 (SUV cap).
- §168(k) 100% bonus on remainder (post-1/19/2025 acquisition).

Example: $90,000 Cadillac Escalade purchased 5/1/2025, 100%
business use:
- §179 (capped): $31,300
- §168(k) bonus on remainder: $58,700
- **Total Year 1 deduction: $90,000**

### Pickups with cargo bed ≥ 6 feet, GVWR > 6,000 lbs

**NOT** subject to the §179 SUV cap (per **IRS Notice 2018-59**
and Reg. §1.179-1(c)(3)). The full §179 limit ($2.5M for 2025)
applies. Common qualifying vehicles: Ford F-250 with 8-foot bed,
Ram 2500/3500, Silverado 2500/3500, full-size cargo vans.

Example: $75,000 Ford F-250 with standard 8-ft bed, 100%
business use, placed in service 6/1/2025:
- §179 expense: $75,000 (within $2.5M limit, not capped at
  $31,300).
- **Total Year 1 deduction: $75,000.**

### Cargo vans, work trucks, vehicles > 14,000 GVWR

NOT subject to SUV cap. Full §179 limit applies. Examples:
Mercedes Sprinter, Ford Transit cargo van (no rear seating),
commercial work trucks, semi-tractors.

### Tax-credit interaction

EV credits (§30D for new clean vehicles, §25E for used clean
vehicles) **reduce the depreciable basis** for §179 / §168(k)
purposes by the credit amount. The credit and depreciation
**stack** but the basis is reduced.

## Mechanics of accountable plan reimbursement

### Statutory framework (same as home office)

- **§62(a)(2)(A)** — accountable plan reimbursements excluded
  from employee gross income.
- **Reg. §1.62-2(c)** — three requirements: business connection,
  substantiation, return of excess.
- **Reg. §1.62-2(c)(2)(ii)** — reimbursable expenses include
  those listed in §§161 through 199, which includes §179 and
  §168 depreciation.
- **Reg. §1.179-1(a) and (e)** — §179 election by the employer
  (S corp) does not require the employer to hold title; only
  requires the property be predominantly used in the
  employer's trade or business and the employer to bear the
  cost (which is satisfied by reimbursement).

### What the corporation reimburses

When the employee owns the vehicle but uses it for corporate
business, the corporation reimburses the **business-use
percentage** of:

**Capital cost (reimbursed in year of acquisition under §179 /
§168(k)):**
- §179 expense election (subject to per-asset and aggregate
  limits).
- §168(k) bonus depreciation on remaining basis after §179.
- Regular MACRS depreciation on remaining basis after §179 +
  §168(k).

**Operating expenses (reimbursed in the period incurred):**
- Fuel.
- Repairs and maintenance.
- Insurance allocable to business use.
- License, registration, personal property tax.
- Garage rent (if applicable).
- Tires.
- Tolls and parking (separately substantiated, 100%-business
  if business-related).

**Alternative: standard mileage rate (instead of actual).** The
corporation may reimburse using the IRS standard mileage rate
instead of actual expenses. **2025 standard mileage rate: $0.70/
mile** (Rev. Proc. 2024-X — verify current). Mileage rate
includes **all operating expenses + depreciation**; cannot also
reimburse §179 or §168(k). Useful for vehicles already past their
optimal §179 / §168(k) year, or where actual-expense tracking is
impractical.

### Document your reimbursement (per PLR 200930029)

The owner-employee must submit an **enhanced expense report**
including:

1. **Receipt for the vehicle purchase** showing the owner's name
   on title and the cost of the vehicle.
2. **Statement of recognition** by the owner that:
   - The §179 / §168(k) reimbursement reduces the owner's basis
     in the vehicle for purposes of gain/loss on subsequent
     disposition.
   - The §179 deduction reimbursed by the corporation reduces
     the **annual §179 limit** that applies to the owner-
     employee personally for **other property** placed in
     service (i.e., the corporation's §179 deduction is taxed
     to the owner via K-1 reduction in income, but the owner
     also has personal §179 limits if they own other Schedule
     C property).
   - If business use of the vehicle drops to **50% or less**,
     the owner-employee will reimburse the corporation for
     required recapture of §179 / §168(k) deductions in excess
     of straight-line depreciation. (Recapture rule expires when
     5 years have passed since placed in service or upon sale/
     trade.)
3. **Mileage log** (contemporaneous) proving business-use
   percentage. Per **§274(d)** and Reg. §1.274-5T, the log must
   include:
   - Date of each trip.
   - Destination.
   - Business purpose.
   - Miles driven for business.
4. **Invoices and receipts** for operating expenses if actual-
   expense reimbursement is used.
5. **Annual statement** of total business and personal mileage,
   business-use percentage, and certification of >50% business
   use.

### Reporting on Forms 1120-S / 1120

The corporation deducts the reimbursed amounts in **"Other
deductions"** (Form 1120-S Line 19; Form 1120 Line 26). Two
labeling choices in practice:

1. **"Vehicle expenses"** — clean and recognizable.
2. **"Employee reimbursed expenses"** — alternative.

The §179 / §168(k) component does NOT separately appear on Form
4562 unless the corporation itself owns the vehicle (which it
doesn't in this scenario). The deduction flows entirely through
the accountable-plan reimbursement line.

## Common pitfalls

1. **Failure to bear the cost as a corporation.** Reg.
   §1.179-1(e) requires the corporation to bear the cost of the
   §179 property. **Reimbursement of the employee satisfies
   this** (PLR 200930029), but the reimbursement must be
   **timely** and **substantiated**. Lump-sum year-end
   "reimbursements" without contemporaneous documentation invite
   challenge.
2. **Business use ≤ 50%.** §280F(b)(1) and (b)(2) — if business
   use is 50% or less in the placed-in-service year, **§179 is
   completely disallowed** and bonus depreciation is reduced.
   In subsequent years, if business use drops to 50% or less,
   **recapture** is triggered for excess depreciation over
   straight-line ADS.
3. **Inadequate mileage log.** *Royster v. Commissioner*, T.C.
   Memo. 2010-16 — without a contemporaneous log meeting
   §274(d) standards, the IRS may disallow ALL business vehicle
   deductions. Apps (MileIQ, Triplog) provide contemporaneous
   GPS records.
4. **§179(d)(2) related-party rules.** Property acquired from
   a related party (per §267(b)) does NOT qualify for §179.
5. **Multiple vehicles.** If the corporation reimburses §179 on
   multiple vehicles, the §179 phase-out and the SUV cap apply
   to the corporation's aggregate — but the SUV cap is per
   vehicle, not per business.
6. **Spouse on title.** Joint titling with a spouse who is not
   an employee of the corporation does not disqualify
   reimbursement, but documentation should be clear that the
   employee's portion is what's used for business.
7. **State conformity.** Many states do not conform to §179 limits
   or §168(k) bonus. **California:** §179 capped at $25,000 (CA
   §17250); no conformity to bonus depreciation. **Texas
   franchise tax:** §179 in COGS allowed; bonus not allowed in
   COGS (limited to revenue offset). Check state-by-state.
8. **Sale or trade-in.** When the employee sells the vehicle, the
   reimbursed §179 / §168(k) / depreciation creates **§1245 / §1250
   recapture** at ordinary rates. Owner must track basis (reduced
   by reimbursed depreciation) for proper reporting.
9. **Listed property requirements.** Vehicles are "listed
   property" under §280F(d)(4). Must satisfy regular-and-
   exclusive-business-use threshold and contemporaneous
   substantiation requirements.
10. **Personal use / commuting.** Commuting from home to
    regular workplace is **personal use** (not business). The
    home-office strategy (see related skill `s-corp-home-
    office-reimbursement`) converts the home into the
    "principal place of business" for §280A(c)(1)(A)
    administrative-or-management purposes, eliminating
    commuting miles and increasing the business-use
    percentage substantially.

## Worked example (heavy SUV with home-office combo)

**Facts:**
- Owner-employee of S corp purchases used $50,000 Yukon (GVWR
  7,500 lbs) on 5/15/2025.
- Personal title; corporation reimburses under accountable plan.
- Home office qualifies under §280A(c)(1) and is the
  administrative/management principal place of business. (No
  commuting miles to disallow.)
- Annual mileage: 24,000 total; 22,000 business (92% business
  use because home office eliminates commuting).
- Operating expenses (annual): $4,500 fuel, $1,200 maintenance,
  $1,800 insurance, $300 registration = $7,800.

**Year 1 (2025) reimbursement calculation:**

§179 expense: $31,300 (heavy SUV cap)
§168(k) bonus on remainder: $50,000 - $31,300 = $18,700
Total capital cost reimbursement: $50,000 (× 92% = $46,000
allocable; $4,000 personal-use portion)

Operating expense reimbursement: $7,800 × 92% = $7,176

**Total Year 1 reimbursement: $53,176**

**S corp Form 1120-S Other deductions:** $53,176

**Owner tax savings (assuming 32% federal + 3.8% NIIT on K-1
income passed through):** ~$19,000+ in federal tax savings, plus
state savings depending on jurisdiction.

**Without accountable plan (vehicle deductions on Schedule A):
$0 federal deduction** for years 2018-2025 due to §67(g).
