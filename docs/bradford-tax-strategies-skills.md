# Part A — Home Office Reimbursement (S Corporation)

## A1. The Reimbursement Method (the only method that works)

**Authority:**

- **IRC §62(a)(2)(A)** (reimbursement of business expenses excluded
  from gross income)
- **IRC §67(g)** (TCJA suspension of miscellaneous itemized
  deductions for tax years 2018–2025)
- **IRC §280A(c)(1)** (home office requirements: exclusive use,
  regular use, principal place of business or place to meet clients)
- **IRC §280A(c)(6)** (rental of home to employer disallows
  deductions for the rented portion)
- **IRC §1(h)(6)(A)** (unrecaptured §1250 gain on sale — 25% max
  rate)
- **Treas. Reg. §1.62-2(c)** (accountable plan rules)
- **Treas. Reg. §1.62-2(d)(1)** (employer deduction for accountable-
  plan reimbursements)
- **Hamacher v. Commissioner**, 94 T.C. 348 (1990) (convenience-of-
  employer test)
- **Commissioner v. Soliman**, 506 U.S. 168 (1993) (principal place
  of business — TCJA legislatively modified Soliman by enacting
  §280A(c)(1) flush language permitting administrative use)
- **PMTA 00431_7138** (IRS Program Manager Technical Assistance:
  §280A(c)(6) bars employee deductions for renting portion of home
  to employer; S corp itself can still deduct its rent payment as
  §162 expense)
- **TAM published 6/17/1988** (employees may not deduct business
  expenses attributable to rental of home to employer corporation)
- **Sinopoli v. Commissioner**, T.C. Memo 2023-105 (Augusta-rule-
  style rental between owner and S corp largely disallowed for lack
  of substantiation and unreasonable rental rate)

### Purpose

Provide an S corporation owner-employee with the full economic
benefit of the home-office deduction by routing the expense
through the corporation as an **accountable plan reimbursement**.
This is the **only method that works** post-TCJA, because the two
alternative methods (renting the home to the corporation; claiming
the home office on Form 2106 as an unreimbursed employee expense)
each fail.

### Why the alternatives fail

**Why renting the home to the corporation fails.** §280A(c)(6)
disallows any deduction otherwise allowable for the rented portion
when the lessor-employee uses the rented portion in performing
services as an employee of the lessee-employer. The S corporation
*can* deduct the rent it pays, but the owner-employee then
recognizes the full rental income on Schedule E and gets no
offsetting deduction (no depreciation, no utilities allocation, no
mortgage interest allocation against rental income). Net effect:
the rent payment is a wash — money is moved from one hand to the
other with no tax benefit. PMTA 00431_7138 confirms this rule.

**Why Form 2106 unreimbursed employee expenses fail.** TCJA §11045
(IRC §67(g)) suspended **all** miscellaneous itemized deductions
subject to the 2% AGI floor for tax years beginning after December
31, 2017 and before January 1, 2026. Unreimbursed employee
business expenses fall in this category. Even if the suspension
expires post-2025, the historical pre-TCJA limitations (2% AGI
floor, must itemize, AMT preference) eliminated most of the
benefit. Through 2025, the deduction is **zero**.

### The right way: reimbursement under an accountable plan

An accountable plan under Treas. Reg. §1.62-2(c) requires that:

1. The expense have a **business connection** (incurred in
   connection with services performed as an employee).
2. The employee **substantiate** the expense to the employer
   within a reasonable period.
3. The employee **return any excess** within a reasonable period.

When all three conditions are met:

- The reimbursement is **excluded from the employee's gross
  income** (IRC §62(a)(2)(A); Treas. Reg. §1.62-2(d)(1)).
- The corporation **deducts** the full reimbursement as an
  ordinary and necessary business expense (Treas. Reg. §1.62-2(d)(1)).
- No payroll taxes apply.
- No reporting on Form W-2.

For the home office, eligible reimbursable expenses include the
business-use share of:

- Mortgage interest
- Property taxes
- Utilities (electric, gas, water, sewer, trash)
- Homeowners insurance
- HOA dues
- Repairs / maintenance allocable to office
- Depreciation (straight-line, 39-year nonresidential real)
- Cleaning service
- Internet (business-use portion)
- Pest control allocable to office
- Security system

### Substantiation requirements

Per IRC §280A(c)(1) and Treas. Reg. §1.280A, the employee must
substantiate (and retain backup for):

1. **Proof of expense** (utility bills, mortgage statements, etc.)
2. **Exclusive use** — attestation + photo of setup; office space
   used only for business.
3. **Regular use** — attestation + log of business use hours.
4. **Business use** — type of activity performed in the office.
5. **Convenience of employer** — required by Hamacher v. Comm'r
   (94 T.C. 348). Employer must demonstrate the home office is for
   the employer's convenience, not merely the employee's.

### Hamacher convenience-of-employer test

Per Hamacher and progeny, the home office is for the convenience
of the employer when *any one* of the following applies:

1. Maintenance of the home office is a **condition of employment**.
2. The home office is **necessary for the functioning** of the
   employer's business (e.g., the employer has no other office for
   this employee).
3. The home office is **necessary to allow the employee to perform
   duties properly**.

If the home office is "purely a matter of personal convenience,
comfort, or economy with respect to the employee," it fails. The
practitioner should produce a written corporate resolution
documenting why the home office is for the corporation's
convenience.

### Mechanics

1. **Adopt accountable plan resolution** at the corporate level
   (board minutes / written corporate policy).
2. **Document the §280A(c)(1) exclusive/regular/business use**
   characteristics with photos, floor plan, and written attestation.
3. **Document Hamacher convenience-of-employer** test in a
   corporate resolution.
4. **Submit annual expense report** to corporation with backup
   documentation.
5. **Corporation reimburses** within reasonable period.
6. **Corporation deducts** under "Office Expense" or "Office
   Space" line on Form 1120-S (typically Other Deductions, line 19,
   but Office Expenses on a custom line works equally well).

### Tax return preparation

- **On Form 1120-S:** Reimbursement deducted as "office space" or
  "office expense" in the other deductions section.
- **On the owner's Form 1040:** No income inclusion for the
  reimbursement. The owner *reduces* the itemized deductions on
  Schedule A by the amounts reimbursed for mortgage interest and
  property taxes (since the corporation has effectively paid those
  for the owner via reimbursement).
- **Permanent file:** Track the reimbursed straight-line
  depreciation. This depreciation reduces the owner's basis in the
  home; on sale, this reimbursed depreciation is subject to
  unrecaptured §1250 gain at a maximum 25% rate under §1(h)(6)(A).

### Pitfalls

- **Pre-TCJA versus post-TCJA tax planning.** Under prior law
  (pre-2018), some practitioners favored the rent-to-corporation
  approach because the §280A(c)(6) limitation was sometimes
  ignored. Post-TCJA, the rent approach is even less attractive
  because (i) §280A(c)(6) still disallows the offsetting
  deductions, and (ii) the owner pays SE tax / NIIT on the rental
  income (depending on classification).
- **Sinopoli v. Commissioner** (T.C. Memo 2023-105) — the Tax
  Court denied most of an S corporation's home-office-related
  rental deductions because the rental amount was not supported
  by fair-rental-value documentation. While Sinopoli involves the
  Augusta-rule context (§280A(g)), the substantiation lessons
  apply to any owner-corporation expense reimbursement: keep
  documentary proof, calculate amounts at arm's length, and
  retain backup.
- The **"convenience of employer"** documentation is often the
  weak link. A corporate resolution stating *why* the home office
  is required for the corporation (no other office, employee must
  be available remotely, etc.) protects the deduction.
- **Multiple home offices** are permitted if each meets §280A(c)(1)
  separately; pro-rate the reimbursement.
- **Sale of home** triggers §1(h)(6) unrecaptured §1250 gain on
  reimbursed depreciation; the taxpayer cannot avoid this by
  failing to take depreciation. The §121 exclusion is reduced by
  the depreciation actually reimbursed.

# Part B — Personal Vehicle Used for Corp Business

## B1. Reimbursing the Owner for a Personally-Titled Vehicle (Section 179 + Bonus + Accountable Plan)

**Authority:**

- **IRC §62(a)(2)(A)**; **Treas. Reg. §1.62-2** (accountable plan)
- **IRC §67(g)** (TCJA suspension of miscellaneous itemized
  deductions for 2018-2025)
- **IRC §168(k)** (additional first-year depreciation / bonus)
- **IRC §168(k)(7)** (election out of bonus)
- **IRC §179** (election to expense)
- **IRC §179(b)(5)** (SUV cap)
- **IRC §280F** (luxury auto cap; 6,000-pound GVWR exemption)
- **Treas. Reg. §1.179-1(a), (e)** (eligibility — taxpayer "incurs
  the cost"; title not required)
- **Rev. Proc. 87-57** (depreciation tables)
- **Rev. Proc. 2010-51** (standard mileage rate procedures)
- **Notice 2026-11** (post-OBBBA §168(k) interim guidance)
- **Pub. L. 119-21** (OBBBA), §70301 (permanent 100% bonus
  depreciation for property acquired after January 19, 2025)
- **PLR 200930029** (Section 179 reimbursement permitted for
  vehicle titled in owner's personal name)

### Purpose

Provide the corporation owner the full deduction for a vehicle
**titled in the owner's personal name** but used for corporate
business. As with the home office, the only method that captures
full deductibility is **accountable plan reimbursement**. The
corporation reimburses Section 179 expensing, bonus depreciation,
MACRS depreciation, and operating expenses — all flowing through
to the corporation as deductions. The reimbursement is tax-free to
the owner-employee.

### Why the alternative fails

Same three strikes as the home-office Form 2106 trap:

1. **AMT preference** — pre-TCJA, miscellaneous itemized
   deductions were AMT preference items.
2. **Itemization required** — pre-TCJA, taxpayer had to itemize
   to use Form 2106 deductions.
3. **2% AGI floor** — pre-TCJA, deductions were reduced by 2%
   of AGI.
4. **Post-TCJA** — Form 2106 unreimbursed employee expense
   deductions are **suspended entirely** for 2018-2025 under
   §67(g).

### The right way: corporate reimbursement under an accountable plan

The corporation reimburses the owner for:

- **Section 179 expensing** of the vehicle
- **Bonus depreciation** (100% post-OBBBA for property acquired
  after 1/19/2025)
- **MACRS depreciation** (regular)
- **Operating expenses** (gas, insurance, repairs, registration,
  parking, tolls)

Per Treas. Reg. §1.179-1(a) and (e), Section 179 requires that
the corporation **incur the cost** for qualifying property used
predominantly in the conduct of business. **Title in the
corporation's name is NOT required.** PLR 200930029 confirms IRS
acceptance of this treatment: an S corporation properly reimbursed
the owner for Section 179 expense on a vehicle titled in the
owner's personal name, and the IRS allowed the deduction.

### Section 179 limits (2025)

| Item | 2025 Limit |
|---|---|
| Aggregate §179 deduction | $2,500,000 |
| Phase-out threshold | $4,000,000 |
| Heavy SUV cap (§179(b)(5)) | $31,300 |

**For 2026:** verify under Rev. Proc. (typically issued late prior
year); aggregate limit indexed.

### Bonus depreciation (post-OBBBA) `[OBBBA]`

OBBBA §70301 **permanently restored** 100% bonus depreciation for
qualified property **acquired after January 19, 2025**, and placed
in service. Property acquired on or before January 19, 2025
generally follows the prior phase-down (40% for 2025 absent
contractual binding date relief).

**Per Notice 2026-11**, post-OBBBA §168(k) tracks the existing
Reg. §1.168(k)-2 framework for eligibility, timing, and elections.

### §280F luxury auto cap interaction

- **Passenger vehicles ≤ 6,000 lbs GVWR**: subject to §280F
  luxury auto cap. 2025 first-year cap is **$20,200** with bonus,
  **$12,200** without bonus. Year 2: $19,600. Year 3: $11,800.
  Year 4 and after: $7,060.
- **Heavy SUVs (6,000 lbs < GVWR ≤ 14,000 lbs)**: NOT subject to
  §280F luxury auto cap. §179 deduction capped at **$31,300**
  (2025) per §179(b)(5) for SUVs primarily designed to carry
  passengers. **No SUV bonus depreciation cap** — full bonus
  available for amounts above the §179 SUV cap if vehicle was
  acquired after 1/19/2025.
- **Trucks and vans (no rear seating, GVWR > 6,000)** and
  **vehicles with GVWR > 14,000 lbs**: not subject to §179 SUV
  cap or §280F luxury auto cap. Full §179 deduction up to
  aggregate cap; full bonus depreciation.

**Common heavy SUVs (>6,000 lbs GVWR)**: Cadillac Escalade,
Chevrolet Tahoe/Suburban, Ford Expedition, GMC Yukon, Lincoln
Navigator, Toyota Sequoia, BMW X7, Mercedes GLS, Audi Q7/Q8,
Lexus LX, certain Range Rovers, Tesla Model X. **Always verify
GVWR on the door-jamb sticker.**

### Section 179 + bonus combination example (heavy SUV)

Owner buys a $75,000 used heavy SUV (GVWR 7,400 lbs) on June 1,
2025; 100% business use; acquired after 1/19/2025.

| Step | Calculation | Amount |
|---|---|---|
| §179 SUV cap | $31,300 (max) | $31,300 |
| Remaining basis | $75,000 − $31,300 | $43,700 |
| 100% bonus (OBBBA) | $43,700 × 100% | $43,700 |
| **Total Year 1 deduction** | | **$75,000** |

The corporation reimburses the owner $75,000 in Year 1 (or
multi-year if appropriate) under the accountable plan.

### Operating expenses

Beyond depreciation/§179, reimburse the actual operating costs:

- Gas
- Oil and routine maintenance
- Insurance
- Repairs
- Registration / license / inspection fees
- Tolls and parking
- DMV fees

**Note:** Section 179, bonus, and MACRS depreciation are NOT
allowed when the standard mileage rate is elected (Rev. Proc.
2010-51). The taxpayer must use **actual expense method** to claim
§179/bonus/MACRS reimbursement. Once §179 is taken, the standard
mileage rate is unavailable for that vehicle in subsequent years.

### Documentation — enhanced expense report for personally-titled vehicle

Per PLR 200930029, the expense report submitted by the owner
should include:

1. **Receipt for vehicle purchase** showing owner's name and cost.
2. **Statement of recognition** that reimbursed §179/bonus/MACRS
   depreciation reduces the owner's basis in the vehicle for
   purposes of gain or loss on sale.
3. **Statement of recognition** that the §179 reimbursement
   reduces the owner's overall annual §179 limits by the amount
   reimbursed (the §179 limit applies at the **taxpayer level**
   in aggregate across all the taxpayer's businesses).
4. **Statement of recognition** that if business use drops to 50%
   or less within the recapture period, the owner will reimburse
   the corporation for required recapture under §1245(a)(1).
   (The recapture obligation expires after 5 years for 5-year
   property, or upon sale/trade.)
5. **Mileage log** demonstrating business-use percentage.

A standing accountable plan resolution should reference these
elements as required components of all vehicle reimbursement
expense reports.

### Recordkeeping under §274(d)

§274(d) imposes **strict substantiation requirements** for vehicle
expenses (listed property historically, but no longer "listed"
since 2010 Small Business Jobs Act removed cell phones; vehicles
remain subject). Under Reg. §1.274-5T(b)(6):

- **Amount** of each expense
- **Time** (date of use)
- **Place** (destination)
- **Business purpose**

The Cohan rule does NOT apply to §274(d) substantiation. A missing
mileage log generally results in disallowance of the entire
business-use percentage claim. A contemporaneous log (paper,
spreadsheet, or app such as MileIQ or Everlance) is the safest
approach.

### Tax return reporting

- **On Form 1120-S:** Reimbursement appears in Other Deductions
  (line 19) labeled either "Vehicle Expenses" or "Employee
  Reimbursed Expenses." The §179 deduction does NOT separately
  appear on the corporate return — it flows through as part of
  the reimbursement.
- **On the owner's Form 1040:** No income; no deduction. Track
  vehicle basis reductions in permanent file.
- **Recapture risk:** If business use drops ≤50% in any year of
  the recovery period, the owner must reimburse the corporation
  for the recapture amount under §1245.

### Recapture mechanics

Under §1245(a)(1), if business use drops to ≤50% before the end
of the recovery period:

1. Compute depreciation that would have been allowable under
   straight-line ADS.
2. Subtract from depreciation actually claimed (§179 + bonus +
   MACRS).
3. The difference is **recaptured** as ordinary income to the
   business owner in the year of the drop.
4. The corporation backs out the recaptured amount from its prior
   deduction (or the owner remits cash to the corporation under
   the accountable plan).

After 5 years of post-acquisition holding, recapture risk
generally ends for 5-year property (i.e., most autos, light
trucks).

### Pitfalls

- **§179 limit applies at the taxpayer level**, not the entity
  level (Reg. §1.179-2(b)). If the owner has multiple businesses
  (S corp + sole prop + LLC), the aggregate §179 limit ($2.5M for
  2025) applies in total.
- **Married couples** filing jointly share one §179 aggregate
  limit.
- **PLR 200930029 is not precedential** but is widely followed.
  The mechanics it endorses (accountable plan reimbursement of
  §179 expensed on a personally-titled vehicle) are well
  established.
- **Mileage log is non-negotiable.** Sinopoli v. Commissioner and
  many other recent Tax Court cases confirm that §274(d)
  substantiation cannot be reconstructed.
- **Don't use 2106** under any circumstances during 2018-2025
  (TCJA suspension).
- **Heavy SUV strategy** has remained intact under OBBBA — the
  §179 SUV cap of $31,300 (2025) has not been increased; bonus
  on the residual basis is the meaningful change post-OBBBA.
- **State conformity varies** — some states do not conform to
  bonus depreciation (CA, NJ, others); §179 is generally state-
  conforming. Run state computations separately.

## C1. Reduce S Corp Owner's Wages — Reasonable Compensation

**Authority:**

- **IRC §3121(d)** (employee defined for FICA)
- **IRC §1366** (S corp pass-through)
- **IRC §7436** (Tax Court jurisdiction over employment status /
  reclassification)
- **Treas. Reg. §1.162-7(a)** (reasonable compensation
  requirement)
- **Treas. Reg. §1.162-7(b)(1)** (compensation must be paid
  purely for services)
- **Treas. Reg. §1.162-7(b)(3)** (factors)
- **Mayson Mfg. Co. v. Commissioner**, 178 F.2d 115 (6th Cir.
  1949) (nine-factor framework)
- **Elliotts, Inc. v. Commissioner**, 716 F.2d 1241 (9th Cir.
  1983) (five-factor framework — independent investor test)
- **Sean McAlary Ltd., Inc. v. Commissioner**, T.C. Summ. Op.
  2013-62 (real estate broker reasonable comp recharacterization)
- **Watson v. United States**, 668 F.3d 1008 (8th Cir. 2012)
  (CPA's S corp salary increased from $24K to $91K)
- **Radtke v. United States**, 712 F. Supp. 143 (E.D. Wis. 1989),
  aff'd, 895 F.2d 1196 (7th Cir. 1990) (zero salary, all
  distribution → entirely recharacterized as wages)
- **Davis v. United States**, 74 AFTR 2d 94-5618 (W.D. Mich.
  1994) (similar)
- **David E. Watson, P.C. v. United States**, 757 F. Supp. 2d
  877 (S.D. Iowa 2010), aff'd 668 F.3d 1008 (8th Cir. 2012)

### Purpose

Minimize the wage portion of S corp owner-employee compensation
to reduce employment taxes (12.4% Social Security up to wage
base + 2.9% Medicare uncapped + 0.9% Additional Medicare on wages
> $200K single / $250K MFJ).

### Cross-reference

This strategy is **comprehensively covered in the existing
strategy library Strategy #21** (with the recent enhancement
covering the Mayson 9-factor framework, Elliotts 5-factor
framework, three valuation approaches — Market/Income/Cost,
RCReports tooling, Tax Adviser Oct 2024 update, and the 2025
Social Security wage base of $176,100 / 2026 $184,500).

**Refer to Strategy #21 in the strategy library for full
treatment.**

### Key takeaways for S-corp planners

1. **Pay yourself a reasonable salary** documented by a wage
   study (BLS OEWS data or RCReports report).
2. **Distribute the remainder** as K-1 distributions (not subject
   to FICA).
3. **Avoid the zero-wage trap** (Radtke, Davis) — IRS will
   reclassify all distributions as wages.
4. **§7436** gives the Tax Court jurisdiction to determine
   whether a worker is an employee and reclassify distributions
   as wages.

## C2. Deduct S Corp Owner's Health Insurance Premiums

**Authority:**

- **IRC §162(l)** (self-employed health insurance deduction)
- **IRC §1372** (more-than-2% S corp shareholder treated as
  partner for fringe benefit purposes — no §106 exclusion)
- **Notice 2008-1** (procedure for S corp shareholder health
  insurance deduction)
- **Notice 2015-17** (transitional relief for S corp employer
  payment plans pre-ACA)
- **Rev. Rul. 91-26** (S corp 2%+ shareholders treated as
  partners for fringe benefits)
- **§9831(d)** (QSEHRA — added by 21st Century Cures Act, P.L.
  114-255, 2016)
- **§9802(c) / §2716 PHS Act** (ICHRA framework — Treasury
  Notice 2019-31; final regs under T.D. 9867)

### Purpose

Achieve a **dollar-for-dollar deduction** for the more-than-2%
S corp shareholder's health insurance premiums by routing the
payment through the corporation, including the premium amount in
the shareholder's W-2 wages, then deducting it on Form 1040
Schedule 1, line 17 (self-employed health insurance).

### Mechanics (per Notice 2008-1)

1. **Corporation establishes the health insurance plan** (or pays
   the shareholder's individually-purchased policy directly OR
   reimburses the shareholder for premiums paid out-of-pocket).
2. **Corporation pays the premiums.**
3. **Premium amount is added to the shareholder's W-2 Box 1
   (wages)**, NOT Box 3 (Social Security wages) or Box 5
   (Medicare wages). Reported in Box 14 with a code like "HLTH"
   for clarity.
4. **Corporation deducts** the premium as compensation expense.
5. **Shareholder deducts** the premium on Form 1040 Schedule 1,
   line 17 (self-employed health insurance) — full above-the-line
   deduction.

### Net tax effect

- Corporation deducts premium (W-2 wage expense).
- Shareholder includes premium in income (W-2 wages → 1040 line 1).
- Shareholder deducts premium (Schedule 1 line 17).
- **Net = zero** at the federal level (deduction offsets
  inclusion).
- **FICA/Medicare** is **not** affected (premium added to Box 1
  but not Box 3 or 5).

### Family attribution under §1372 / §318

For purposes of §1372 (treating 2%+ shareholders as partners), the
**§318 attribution rules** apply. A shareholder's spouse, parents,
children, and grandchildren are treated as owning the
shareholder's stock. So a 2%+ shareholder's spouse is treated as
a 2%+ shareholder for fringe benefit purposes.

### ACA market reform interaction

After ACA, **standalone HRAs** that reimburse individually-
purchased premiums are generally prohibited as group health plans
that fail ACA market reforms. **Three exceptions:**

1. **One-employee HRA** (the 105-HRA) — surviving exception
   because ACA requirements apply to plans covering 2+
   participants.
2. **QSEHRA** (Qualified Small Employer HRA) — permitted by 21st
   Century Cures Act (P.L. 114-255, December 13, 2016) for
   employers with <50 FTEs that don't offer group coverage.
3. **ICHRA** (Individual Coverage HRA) — permitted from 2020 by
   Treasury final regs (T.D. 9867); permits employers of any size
   to reimburse individual market premiums and Medicare premiums.

**For S corp 2%+ shareholders:**

- The S corp itself is NOT considered a "group health plan" merely
  because it pays one shareholder's premium and includes the
  amount in W-2 wages (per Notice 2015-17 transitional relief and
  ongoing IRS practice).
- **PCORI fee** (Form 720): QSEHRAs and ICHRAs trigger the PCORI
  fee ($3.22 per covered participant for plan year ending in 2025;
  verify current).
- **2%+ shareholders cannot benefit from QSEHRA** as a tax-
  excluded benefit because §1372 treats them as partners (fringe
  benefits not excluded).

### Diagnostic

| Scenario | Right strategy |
|---|---|
| S corp with only 2%+ shareholder employee | Direct payment + W-2 inclusion + §162(l) deduction |
| S corp with 2%+ shareholder + non-shareholder employees, all eligible for group plan | Group health plan + W-2 add for shareholder |
| S corp with 2%+ shareholder only, no group plan | Reimbursement + W-2 add for shareholder; if other ineligible employees, ICHRA |
| Sole prop / LLC taxed as Schedule C with employee-spouse | §105 HRA → see D1 |

### Pitfalls

- **Failure to add to W-2** → premium not deductible by
  shareholder under §162(l). The deduction requires inclusion in
  wages first.
- **Self-employment tax savings myth** — premium NOT in Box 3/5
  means no FICA savings (would have been 0 anyway since premium
  isn't compensation for services).
- **Spouse's coverage:** Premium for shareholder + spouse + family
  is fully deductible if all are covered under the plan.
- **Pre-65 LTC premiums:** Special §213 caps apply (age-based
  limits indexed annually).

## C3. Employ Your Child (S Corporation)

**Authority:**

- **IRC §3121(b)(3)(A)** — FICA exemption for child under 18
  in **sole proprietorship or parent-only partnership** (NOT
  applicable to S corp)
- **IRC §3306(c)(5)** — FUTA exemption for child under 21 in
  same context (NOT applicable to S corp)
- **IRC §63(c)** — standard deduction (OBBBA increased to
  $15,750 for 2025; $16,100 for 2026 single)
- **IRC §1(g)** — kiddie tax (applies only to unearned income;
  earned income from wages is exempt)
- **IRC §219** — IRA contribution (Roth IRA earned-income
  requirement)

### Purpose

Shift income from the S corp owner's bracket to the child's lower
bracket while creating earned income that qualifies for Roth IRA
contributions. Note that S corp employment **does not** qualify
for the §3121(b)(3)(A) FICA exemption (which requires sole prop
or parent-only partnership). The strategy still works, but with
payroll tax cost.

### Mechanics for S corp

1. **Hire the child as a real employee** doing legitimate work
   (filing, data entry, social media, basic admin).
2. **Pay reasonable wages** for the work performed (use wage
   benchmarks from BLS OEWS or comparable sources).
3. **Issue W-2** with full FICA, Medicare withholding (because
   §3121(b)(3)(A) does NOT apply to S corp).
4. **Withhold payroll taxes** (employer + employee FICA + FUTA).
5. **S corp deducts** wages and employer payroll taxes.
6. **Child files Form 1040** with $0 federal income tax up to
   $15,750 (2025) / $16,100 (2026) standard deduction.
7. **Child contributes earned income to Roth IRA** up to the
   lesser of earnings or annual limit ($7,000 for 2025 under age
   50; verify 2026).

### S corp vs. sole prop comparison

| Item | Sole Prop / Parent-Only Partnership | S Corp |
|---|---|---|
| §3121(b)(3)(A) FICA exemption (under 18) | ✓ Available | ✗ NOT Available |
| §3306(c)(5) FUTA exemption (under 21) | ✓ Available | ✗ NOT Available |
| Income tax shift | ✓ Yes | ✓ Yes |
| Deduction by parent's business | ✓ Schedule C | ✓ Form 1120-S |

For S corp clients, consider the **two-entity structure**: parent
operates a sole prop or partnership for management/administrative
services that pays the child; the S corp pays the management
entity. Requires economic substance (real services, arm's-length
pricing) — see Wipfli and Tom Talks Taxes commentary on §1239
arrangements that fail economic substance review.

### OBBBA enhancement

OBBBA increased the **standard deduction** for 2025 to $15,750
(single/dependent earned-income calculation). Each child can earn
up to that amount and pay zero federal income tax. With multiple
children (3 children × $15,750 = $47,250 of pre-tax wages
shifted), the savings can be substantial even with S corp FICA
costs.

### Documentation

- **W-4, I-9** on file
- **Time records** (date, hours, tasks performed)
- **Reasonable wage benchmark** (BLS OEWS for similar work)
- **Job description** — appropriate for child's age and skill
- **Direct deposit / payroll records**
- **Employment agreement** (recommended; not required)

### Diagnostic

- For S corp owners, evaluate whether forming a **parent-only
  partnership or sole prop** to pay the child for legitimate
  services makes economic sense. The structure must:
  - Have legitimate business activity / services rendered.
  - Pay arm's-length amounts.
  - Maintain books, records, payroll.
  - Survive economic substance scrutiny (Wipfli analysis).

## C4. Sell Home to S Corp Before Converting to Rental

**Authority:**

- **IRC §121** (home sale exclusion — $250K single / $500K MFJ)
- **IRC §121(d)(6)** (depreciation recapture exception)
- **IRC §1239** (related-party gain → ordinary income)
- **IRC §267(c)** (constructive ownership)
- **IRC §1239(c)(1)(A)** (controlled entity = corporation >50%
  owned)
- **IRC §453(g)** (no installment method for related-party
  depreciable property)
- **IRC §1250** (depreciation recapture)
- **IRC §469** (passive activity loss limitations)
- **PLR 8350084** (no §121 prohibition on related-party home sale)
- **Reg. §1.121-1** (§121 mechanics)
- **Reg. §1.1239-1** (§1239 mechanics)
- **Sec. 121 gain exclusion does not offset suspended passive
  losses** — Journal of Accountancy Oct 2014

### Purpose

When the homeowner plans to convert a primary residence into a
rental property, **sell the home to a wholly-owned S corp first**
to:

1. **Use the §121 exclusion** ($250K single / $500K MFJ) to
   eliminate up to that amount of gain from taxation.
2. **Step up the rental property's basis** at the S corp level to
   FMV.
3. **Increase future depreciation deductions** at the S corp
   level.
4. **Convert ordinary income (rental) to capital gain** on
   eventual sale by the S corp (since §1239 only converts gain
   on the original sale, not future sales).

### How it works (4 steps)

**Step 1: Form an S corp** (file Form 2553).

**Step 2: Sell home to S corp** at FMV. The S corp obtains
financing from a third-party lender (mortgage). The owner
receives cash equal to the sale price (potentially less debt
payoff on existing mortgage).

**Step 3: S corp rents the property out.** Rental income +
expenses + depreciation flow through to the owner via K-1.
Subject to §469 passive activity rules unless the owner qualifies
as a real estate professional under §469(c)(7).

**Step 4: §121 eliminates the gain** (up to $250K/$500K).

### §1239 trap and how to avoid it

§1239 converts gain on sale of depreciable property between
related parties to **ordinary income** (no LTCG rates). The S
corp owned 100% by the seller is a **controlled entity** under
§1239(c)(1)(A) (more than 50% owned). The home, in the buyer's
hands, **is depreciable** as residential rental property — so
§1239 applies.

**However:** §1239 only applies to **gain recognized**. To the
extent gain is **excluded** under §121, it is not "recognized"
and §1239 cannot recharacterize it.

**Example (4-year ownership):**

- Original cost: $200,000
- FMV at sale to S corp: $400,000
- Gain: $200,000

Single owner: §121 exclusion = $250,000 (more than $200K gain).
Result: **entire $200,000 gain excluded under §121**; §1239 has
no remaining gain to recharacterize. S corp's basis = $400,000.

If gain **exceeds** the §121 exclusion (e.g., $300K gain, single
owner with $250K exclusion):

- $250,000 excluded under §121.
- $50,000 recognized → §1239 converts to **ordinary income**.

### Critical pitfalls

1. **Passive activity loss interaction.** Per the Journal of
   Accountancy article and Sec. 469(g)(1)(A), §121 gain
   exclusion does NOT offset suspended passive losses. If the
   property has suspended passive losses, those losses do not
   release on the §121-excluded sale because the sale is to a
   related party (not "unrelated party"). Plan accordingly.

2. **§453(g) installment sale prohibition.** If the S corp pays
   for the home over time (note from S corp to owner), §453(g)
   bars installment treatment because the seller and buyer are
   related parties and the property is depreciable. The entire
   gain is recognized in year of sale regardless of payment
   schedule.

3. **§121 ownership and use tests.** Owner must have **owned and
   used** the home as principal residence for periods aggregating
   2 of the last 5 years before sale. Failure → §121 exclusion
   unavailable → entire gain recognized → entire gain ordinary
   under §1239.

4. **§121 anti-flipping rule.** Per §121(b)(3), the exclusion is
   not available if the taxpayer used the §121 exclusion on a
   different home within the prior 2 years.

5. **Mortgage / financing.** The S corp will need a third-party
   mortgage. Owner may need to personally guarantee. Pulling
   $250K-$500K of cash out triggers no taxable event because
   it's the §121-excluded gain.

6. **Stepped-up basis.** After §121 exclusion, the S corp's
   basis is the **purchase price** ($400K in example). Future
   depreciation is computed on this basis. On future S corp sale
   of the rental, gain is §1231 / capital gain; loss is ordinary
   under §1231 — best of both worlds.

7. **Future §121 exclusion availability.** Once the home becomes
   a rental (S-corp-owned), the owner cannot use §121 again on
   the same property. The owner can use §121 on a different
   primary residence in the future.

8. **State tax conformity.** Most states conform to §121 but
   verify; some states have separate rules.

### Mechanics — practitioner's checklist

1. ☐ Form S corp (Form 2553); ensure timing satisfies §121's
   ownership/use test on closing date.
2. ☐ Verify §121 ownership/use test (2 of last 5 years).
3. ☐ Verify no recent §121 use (within prior 2 years).
4. ☐ Obtain qualified appraisal at FMV.
5. ☐ Obtain third-party mortgage financing for S corp.
6. ☐ Close sale at arm's-length terms (FMV price, market interest
   rate, normal closing costs).
7. ☐ Document basis increase in S corp's books to FMV.
8. ☐ Begin depreciating at FMV basis (residential rental,
   27.5-year SL under §168).
9. ☐ Track passive activity loss interaction.
10. ☐ Reduce §121 exclusion by depreciation reimbursed for any
    pre-sale home office portion (§121(d)(6)).

### Cross-references

- **C5 / Part A** (home office reimbursement — separate strategy
  for residence still owned by individual)

## C6. Augusta Rule — Rent Home to S Corp ≤ 14 Days `[§280A(g)]`

**Authority:**

- **IRC §280A(g)** — exclusion of rental income for residence
  rented ≤ 14 days
- **IRC §280A(c)(6)** — rental of home to employer (does NOT
  apply to §280A(g) per its express terms)
- **IRC §280A(d)(3)** — fair rental period rules
- **Treas. Reg. §1.280A-1** through **§1.280A-3**
- **IRC §274(a)** (entertainment facility disallowance — interacts
  with Augusta strategy)
- **Sinopoli v. Commissioner**, T.C. Memo 2023-105 (Augusta-rule
  rentals largely disallowed for unsubstantiated/unreasonable
  rates)

### Purpose

Rent the owner's home to the S corporation for ≤ 14 days per
year for legitimate corporate business purposes. The corporation
deducts the rent. The owner-homeowner **excludes the rent from
income** under §280A(g). Net effect: corporation gets a deduction;
owner gets tax-free cash.

### How §280A(g) works

§280A(g) provides that if a dwelling unit is used by the taxpayer
as a residence and rented to others for fewer than **15 days**
during the taxable year:

1. **No deductions** allowable for the rental use are permitted
   (other than items allowable regardless of the rental — interest,
   taxes).
2. **No rental income** is includable in gross income (this is the
   tax-free part).

### Why §280A(c)(6) does NOT apply

§280A(c)(6) disallows employee deductions for renting a portion
of the home to the employer. **§280A(c)(6) is a deduction-
disallowance rule** for amounts that *would* otherwise be allowed
under §280A(c)(1) or (c)(3). Because §280A(g) supersedes
§280A(c)(1) and (c)(3) by saying "no deductions" anyway, §280A(c)(6)
has nothing to disallow. The owner is not claiming any deductions
for the rental — only the corporation deducts the rent.

PMTA 00431_7138 (2001) and the long-standing IRS practice confirm
this analysis.

### How the corporation deducts

The corporation pays rent at **fair rental value** for legitimate
business use:

- **Quarterly board meetings** (most common)
- **Annual shareholder meetings**
- **Staff retreats**
- **Strategy planning sessions**
- **Training events**
- **Off-site team-building** (caution — see entertainment trap
  below)

The corporation deducts the rent under §162 as ordinary and
necessary business expense.

### Sinopoli pitfall

Sinopoli v. Commissioner, T.C. Memo 2023-105 (Aug 14, 2023)
disallowed most of the S corp's Augusta-rule rent deductions
because:

1. **Unreasonable rental rates.** The S corp paid $290 per
   meeting for use of the owners' home — sometimes 10× a
   comparable hotel meeting room rate.
2. **No supporting documentation.** No meeting agendas, no
   attendee records, no minutes, no fair-rental-value benchmarks.
3. **No evidence of actual use.** No photos, no receipts, no
   attendees.

The Tax Court allowed only $6,000 (out of substantial claimed
amounts) for 2015, and disallowed the entire deduction for 2016
and 2017.

### Documentation requirements

To survive an audit, retain:

1. **Meeting agenda** (purpose, attendees, time).
2. **Sign-in sheet / attendee list.**
3. **Minutes** of any meetings held.
4. **Fair rental value benchmark** (3 comparable hotel/conference
   center quotes for the same date and capacity, kept in the
   file).
5. **Photos** of the meeting/event on the date of rental.
6. **Lease / rental agreement** between owner and corporation
   (for each rental period or annually with date schedule).
7. **Payment records** (check or wire from corp to owner).
8. **Form 1099-MISC?** Not required to issue 1099-MISC under
   §6041 if owner is not in the rental business; some
   practitioners issue anyway for transparency.

### Entertainment facility trap (§274(a))

Post-TCJA, §274(a) disallows **all entertainment expenses** —
including entertainment facilities. Augusta-rule rentals can run
afoul of this if the home is used for entertainment-style events
(parties, social gatherings). The exception:

- **Annual employee holiday party / summer picnic** — §274(e)(4)
  preserves full deductibility (without the 50% cut applicable to
  meals).
- **Business meetings, training, retreats** — these are NOT
  entertainment if the primary purpose is business.

Best practice: document the **business purpose** of each rental
day and avoid using the home for purely social events claimed as
business.

### Related-party rule under §280A(d)(3)

Under §280A(d)(2)(A), use of the home by family members counts
as personal use. But §280A(d)(2)(C) treats family-related use as
rental use **if the rental is at fair rental value** and the home
is used by a family member as their principal residence (which
the owner does anyway). The corporation is **not** in the
related-party group for §280A purposes — so no Section 280A
related-party problem arises for renting to your own corporation.

### Pricing — fair rental value benchmark

The rental rate must be **fair rental value** comparable to what
an unrelated party would pay. Benchmarks:

- **Hotel meeting room rates** for similar capacity and amenity
  level.
- **Airbnb / VRBO daily rates** for similar properties.
- **Local conference center / coworking space day rates.**

Document at least **3 comparable quotes** in the file for each
fiscal year.

### Mechanics — practitioner's checklist

1. ☐ Adopt corporate resolution authorizing Augusta-rule rental
   strategy at start of year.
2. ☐ Calendar the 14-day limit (count carefully if rented to
   others during the year).
3. ☐ Document fair rental value (3 comparable quotes).
4. ☐ Sign rental agreement between owner and corp.
5. ☐ Hold the meetings — keep agenda, attendee list, minutes.
6. ☐ Take photos.
7. ☐ Pay rent from corp to owner.
8. ☐ Track 14-day count.
9. ☐ Year-end: confirm ≤14 days; if exceeded, full §280A vacation
   home rules apply.

### Pitfalls

- **15+ days kills the §280A(g) exclusion.** Day 15 triggers
  full §280A(c)(3) vacation home rules, and rental income becomes
  taxable.
- **Sinopoli** — 14 days at unreasonable rates is the same as
  zero days, audit-wise.
- **Personal residence requirement.** The home must be the
  taxpayer's residence (greater of 14 days or 10% of rental days
  used personally). For most owners, this is satisfied
  automatically.
- **No 1099 from owner needed**, but corporation issues check or
  wire as documentation.
- **State conformity** — most states conform to §280A but
  document for state purposes too.

  reimbursement — these can run concurrently)
- **D5** (Schedule C home office)

## C9. Reimburse Travel Expenses

**Authority:**

- **IRC §162(a)(2)** (travel away from home for trade or
  business)
- **IRC §274(d)** (substantiation: amount, time, place, business
  purpose)
- **IRC §274(c)** (foreign travel)
- **IRC §274(n)** (50% meal limit)
- **IRC §274(k)** (meal substantiation)
- **Treas. Reg. §1.162-2** (travel expense rules)
- **Treas. Reg. §1.274-4** (foreign travel: 7-day / 25% rule)
- **Treas. Reg. §1.274-5** (substantiation)
- **Rev. Proc. 2019-48** (per diem rates and substantiation)
- **Commissioner v. Flowers**, 326 U.S. 465 (1946) ("away from
  home" requirement)
- **Cohan rule unavailable for §274(d)** (Sherman v. Comm'r,
  T.C. Memo 2023-63 — substantiation cannot be reconstructed)

### Purpose

Have the corporation reimburse the owner-employee for legitimate
business travel — same accountable plan mechanics as A1 / B1 —
to avoid the post-TCJA §67(g) suspension of unreimbursed
employee expenses.

### Reimbursable travel expenses

- **Transportation**: airfare, train, mileage (auto), rental car,
  taxi/Uber/Lyft
- **Lodging**: hotel, Airbnb (away from home only)
- **Meals**: 50% deductible to corporation; 100% reimbursable
- **Tips, baggage fees, parking, tolls**
- **Conference / seminar fees**
- **Business-related laundry, communication, and incidentals**

### Substantiation under §274(d)

For each travel expense:

- **Amount** of each separate expenditure
- **Time** (dates of departure and return; days away from home;
  days on business activity)
- **Place** (destinations and locations of travel)
- **Business purpose** (specific purpose of the travel)

### Domestic vs. foreign

| Type | Test | Rule |
|---|---|---|
| **Domestic** | Majority-of-days rule | If majority of days are business → 100% transportation deductible. Lodging/meals deductible only for business days |
| **Foreign ≤ 7 days** | §274(c)(2)(A) exception | Full transportation deductible if ≤ 1 week (excluding day of departure) |
| **Foreign > 7 days, < 25% personal** | §274(c)(2)(B) exception | Full transportation deductible if non-business < 25% of total days |
| **Foreign > 7 days, ≥ 25% personal** | §274(c) general rule | Transportation pro-rated by business days / total days |

- **Strategy library** travel-related strategies

### Pitfalls

- **Cohan rule unavailable** — substantiation cannot be estimated

- **Spousal travel** (§274(m)(3)) — not deductible unless spouse
  is also a bona fide employee with a business purpose.

- **Conventions outside North American area** — §274(h) imposes

- **Cruise ship conventions** — §274(h)(2) imposes specific

## C10. Cell Phone Fringe Benefit `[§132(e); Notice 2011-72]`

**Authority:**

- **IRC §132(a)(3)** (working condition fringe — exclusion from
  gross income)
- **IRC §132(d)** (working condition fringe defined)
- **IRC §132(a)(4)** (de minimis fringe — exclusion)
- **IRC §132(e)** (de minimis fringe defined)
- **§2043 of the Small Business Jobs Act of 2010** (Pub. L.
  111-240) (removed cell phones from listed property under §280F)
- **Notice 2011-72** (cell phones provided primarily for
  noncompensatory business reasons → working condition + de
  minimis fringe exclusion)
- **Treas. Reg. §1.132-1, §1.132-5, §1.132-6**
- **IRS SBSE-04-0911-083** (memo for field examination on
  reimbursement of personal cell phone for business use)

### Purpose

Provide the owner-employee with a tax-free cell phone benefit by
characterizing the corporate-provided phone (or reimbursement of
the owner's personal phone) as a **working condition fringe** for
business use and a **de minimis fringe** for personal use.

### How it works

Under Notice 2011-72:

1. **Corporation provides cell phone** primarily for
   **noncompensatory business reasons**, OR **reimburses owner**
   for owner's personal phone (per IRS SBSE-04-0911-083).
2. **Business use** is excluded from owner-employee income as a
   §132(d) working condition fringe (would be deductible by
   owner under §162 if personally paid).
3. **Personal use** is excluded from owner-employee income as a
   §132(e) de minimis fringe (because the corporation provided
   the phone for noncompensatory business reasons).
4. **Substantiation requirement deemed satisfied** for §132(d)
   purposes — owner does NOT need to track business vs. personal
   minutes / data.
5. **Corporation deducts** the full cost as ordinary §162
   expense.

### Noncompensatory business reasons (Notice 2011-72)

Examples:

- The employer must be able to reach the employee at all times
  for work-related emergencies.
- The employee must be available to speak with clients at any
  time outside the office.
- The employee must speak with clients in different time zones
  at all times.

The phone must NOT be provided primarily as a "perk" or for the
employee's compensation — that defeats the noncompensatory
business reason.

### Mechanics for S corp

**Option 1: Corporation purchases the phone and pays the bill.**

- Phone is corporate asset (or expensed under §179 / de minimis
  safe harbor).
- Monthly bill paid by corporation directly.
- No income to owner; full deduction to corporation.

**Option 2: Owner purchases personal phone; corporation
reimburses.**

- Per IRS SBSE-04-0911-083 (the field memo accompanying Notice
  2011-72), reimbursement of the **business portion** of an
  employee's personal phone is treated as a working condition
  fringe (no income, no substantiation).
- Reimbursement of full personal cost is generally treated as
  nontaxable per the same memo, *provided* the phone is used
  primarily for noncompensatory business reasons.
- Best practice: corporation has accountable plan; owner
  submits cell phone bill; corporation reimburses.

### Pitfalls

- **Cash allowances** — historically NOT excludable as de minimis
  fringe (Reg. §1.132-6(c)). Notice 2011-72 carves out cell
  phones specifically; cash allowances for cell phones are
  generally ok per the Field Memo when tied to the owner's
  actual cell phone bill.
- **Personal phone NOT used primarily for business** — cannot
  use the strategy. If <50% business use, the phone is not
  primarily for noncompensatory business reasons.
- **Notice 2011-72 limited to cell phones** — does NOT extend
  to other electronics (tablets, laptops, etc.) by its express
  terms. Other working condition fringe analysis applies for
  those (Reg. §1.132-5).
- **Documentation:** Keep the corporate resolution stating that
  the phone is provided for noncompensatory business reasons.

### Cross-references

- **D10** (Schedule C cell phone — same authority)
- **D6** (de minimis fringe benefits generally)

## D1. §105-HRA Spouse-Employee Health Plan

**Authority:**

  expenses — including employee fringe benefits)
- **IRC §213(d)** (medical care expense definition)
- **Rev. Rul. 71-588** (sole proprietor's spouse-employee plan
  reimbursing family medical expenses is deductible)
- **PLR 9409006** (additional support for spouse-employee 105 HRA)
- **Notice 2002-45** (HRA framework)
- **§9831(d)** — QSEHRA (added by 21st Century Cures Act, P.L.
  114-255)
- **§9802(c) / Treasury Notice 2019-31; T.D. 9867** — ICHRA
- **ACA market reform rules** — generally prohibit standalone HRAs
  for 2+ participants; one-employee HRA exception preserved
- **Notice 2015-17** (transitional relief; one-person HRA
  surviving exception)
- **Albers v. Commissioner**, T.C. Memo 2007-144 (spouse-employee
  HRA upheld with proper documentation)

### Purpose

For a Schedule C taxpayer (sole prop or single-member LLC taxed
as sole prop) with **a spouse who is the sole eligible employee**,
establish a §105 Health Reimbursement Arrangement (HRA) covering
the spouse-employee on a **family** basis. The plan reimburses
the spouse's family medical expenses (including the proprietor
under "family" coverage). Effect:

1. **Plan reimburses family medical expenses** including the
   proprietor's own medical costs.
2. **Reimbursements deductible by the business** under §162.
3. **Reimbursements excluded from spouse-employee's income**
   under §105(b).
4. **Reimbursements excluded from FICA/FUTA** as employee fringe
   benefits.
5. **Self-employment tax savings** because the deduction reduces
   Schedule C income.

### Why the one-employee 105-HRA survived ACA

ACA market reform rules (§9815, §2711 PHS Act) prohibit standalone
HRAs that fail annual/lifetime benefit caps and other essential
benefit requirements. These rules apply to **group health plans**
with 2+ participants. The **one-employee HRA** is excepted from
group health plan rules (Notice 2015-17 transitional relief and
ongoing IRS practice). When the only eligible employee is the
spouse, the plan covers one participant (the employee) on a
family basis — covering the proprietor as "family member" — but
the employee count remains one.

If the business has any **other eligible employees besides the
spouse**, the one-employee 105-HRA fails ACA. In that case, use
**QSEHRA** (50 or fewer FTEs, no group health offered) or **ICHRA**
(any size).

### Mechanics

1. **Hire spouse** as legitimate W-2 employee performing actual
   work in the business. Spouse must:
   - Have a real job description.
   - Work real hours.
   - Be paid reasonable wages.
2. **Adopt §105 HRA plan document** specifying:
   - Eligibility (only the spouse-employee is eligible).
   - Reimbursable expenses (any §213(d) medical expenses for
     spouse and family).
   - Annual limit (recommended: cap to limit catastrophic
     exposure).
   - Substantiation requirements.
3. **Spouse's family coverage** under the plan covers the
   proprietor as "family member" of the spouse-employee.
4. **Spouse submits medical expense claims** to the business.
5. **Business reimburses** spouse for family medical expenses
   ($213(d)-qualifying).
6. **Business deducts** the reimbursement on Schedule C as
   "Employee benefit programs."
7. **Spouse excludes** the reimbursement under §105(b).

### What §213(d) covers (broad)

- Health insurance premiums (including ACA marketplace policies
  and Medicare)
- Dental, vision, hearing
- Prescription drugs
- Doctor visits, hospitalization
- Long-term care (subject to age-based caps)
- Mental health services
- Eyeglasses, contacts
- Acupuncture, chiropractic
- Smoking cessation, weight loss (if for specific medical
  condition)
- Some home modifications for medical reasons

### Pitfalls

- **Multiple businesses aggregation rule.** Per §105(h) and
  general aggregation principles, all employees in the
  proprietor's businesses (including spouse's businesses)
  are treated as employees of the proprietor for §105 plan
  purposes. If the spouse also operates her own business with
  employees, those employees count toward the one-employee limit.
- **PCORI fee** (Form 720) is generally required for HRAs.
  Currently $3.22 per covered participant for plan year ending
  in 2025.
- **Spouse must be a bona fide employee.** Employment must be
  legitimate (real work, reasonable wages, payroll records,
  W-2). The IRS challenges sham spouse-employee arrangements.
- **Reasonable compensation requirement.** Wage paid to spouse
  must be reasonable; the plan reimbursement is not "extra"
  compensation.
- **Schedule C SE tax savings:** The HRA reimbursement reduces
  Schedule C net income, which reduces SE tax (15.3% on net
  earnings up to wage base; 2.9% above).

### Comparison vs. self-employed health insurance deduction

| Strategy | Where deducted | Reduces SE income? |
|---|---|---|
| **§162(l) self-employed health insurance** | Schedule 1 Line 17 | ✗ NO (above-the-line, no SE effect) |
| **§105 HRA spouse-employee** | Schedule C "Employee benefit programs" | ✓ YES (reduces SE base) |

For a sole prop with self-only coverage and no spouse-employee,
§162(l) is the only option. For a sole prop with spouse who can
legitimately be an employee, §105 HRA is **substantially
better** because it reduces SE tax.

## D2. Employ Your Child (Schedule C — Best FICA/FUTA Treatment)

**Authority:**

- **IRC §3121(b)(3)(A)** — FICA exemption for child under 18 in
  parent's sole prop or parent-only partnership
- **IRC §3306(c)(5)** — FUTA exemption for child under 21 in
  same context
- **IRC §63(c)** — standard deduction (OBBBA: $15,750 for 2025;
  $16,100 for 2026 single)
- **IRC §1(g)** — kiddie tax (applies to unearned income only)
- **IRC §219** — Roth IRA earned-income requirement
- **IRS Pub. 15** — employer's tax guide
- **IRS Pub. 929** — children's tax issues

### Purpose

Schedule C and parent-only-partnership context offers the
**best** child-employment tax treatment because BOTH:

1. **FICA exempt** under §3121(b)(3)(A) for child under 18.
2. **FUTA exempt** under §3306(c)(5) for child under 21.

Plus the federal income tax shift to the child's lower bracket
(or zero, if wages ≤ standard deduction).

### Cross-reference

This strategy is **comprehensively covered in the existing
strategy library Strategy #49** (hire your kids). The library
treatment includes:

- §3121(b)(3)(A) FICA exemption
- §3306(c)(5) FUTA exemption
- OBBBA standard deduction increase ($15,750 / $16,100)
- Multi-business structure to preserve FICA exemption when
  parent has S corp
- Documentation requirements
- Roth IRA contribution mechanics
- Child labor law considerations

### Quick mechanics summary

1. **Hire child** as legitimate employee (real work, real hours).
2. **Pay reasonable wages** for the work (BLS OEWS benchmarks).
3. **W-2 with $0 FICA / $0 FUTA** if child < 18 in parent-only
   sole prop / parent-only partnership.
4. **Withhold income tax** (child likely owes $0 if wages ≤
   standard deduction).
5. **Schedule C deducts wages** as employee compensation; reduces
   SE tax base.
6. **Child files Form 1040** with $0 federal income tax up to
   standard deduction.
7. **Child contributes earned income to Roth IRA** up to
   contribution limit.

### Pitfalls

- **S corp doesn't qualify** for §3121(b)(3)(A) — see C3.
- **Partnership with non-parent partner** disqualifies (must be
  parent-only partnership).
- **State labor laws** apply (hours, types of work, work
  permits).
- **Documentation must be airtight** — IRS challenges sham
  arrangements (no work performed, allowance disguised as wage).

## D3. Employ Your Spouse (with §105 HRA)

**Authority:**

- All authorities cited under D1
- **IRC §3121(b)(3)** (family employment FICA rules — spouse
  generally subject to FICA)
- **IRC §3306(c)(5)** (FUTA exemption — spouse generally subject
  to FUTA)
- **Albers v. Commissioner**, T.C. Memo 2007-144

### Purpose

Hire the spouse as a legitimate employee in the Schedule C
business, with the **primary purpose** being to enable the §105
HRA strategy under D1 (family medical expense reimbursement).
Secondary benefits include income shifting (limited because
spouse is generally in same household tax bracket) and Social
Security earnings credit for spouse.

### Differences from D2 (hire your child)

- **Spouse IS subject to FICA** in sole proprietorship (§3121
  does not exempt spouse from FICA).
- **Spouse IS subject to FUTA** above $7,000 wage base.
- **Spouse is NOT subject to self-employment tax** on wages
  (since paid as W-2 employee, not as partnership distribution).

### Mechanics

1. **Adopt accountable plan / §105 HRA plan document.**
2. **Hire spouse** with real job description, hours, and wages.
3. **Pay reasonable wages** that justify the §105 HRA benefit
   (low wages + high reimbursement may flag IRS as disguised
   draw).
4. **Withhold FICA/Medicare** (spouse pays employee share;
   business pays employer share).
5. **W-2** issued to spouse.
6. **Spouse submits §213(d) medical expenses** for family
   coverage.
7. **Business reimburses** through §105 HRA; deduction taken on
   Schedule C "Employee benefit programs."
8. **Reimbursement excluded** from spouse's W-2 income.

### Reasonable wage guidance

The wage paid to the spouse must be **reasonable** for the work
performed AND should be enough to make the §105 HRA defensible:

- Pay the spouse at least the **fair market rate** for the work
  performed.
- Avoid paying minimum wage with $20K HRA reimbursement (looks
  like disguised draw).
- Document hours, tasks, and how the wage was determined.

## D4. Rent from Your Spouse — Schedule E to Avoid SE Tax

**Authority:**

- **IRC §1402(a)(1)** (rental real estate excluded from SE
  income, with limited exceptions)
- **IRC §1402(a)** (NESE definition)
- **IRC §469(c)(2)** (rental real estate is generally passive)
- **Treas. Reg. §1.1402(a)-4(c)** (services rendered to occupant
  rule)
- **Bobo v. Commissioner**, 70 T.C. 706 (1978) (services
  "usually or customarily provided" excluded; trailer park
  laundry/utility not subject to SE)
- **CCA 202151005** (December 23, 2021 — vacation rental with
  substantial services subject to SE tax)
- **Rev. Rul. 57-108** (vacation rental with concierge services
  → SE tax applies)
- **§469(c)(7)** (real estate professional rules)

### Purpose

Reduce self-employment tax on Schedule C income by:

1. Owning a building (e.g., the office building) personally (or
   in spouse's name).
2. **Spouse rents the building to the proprietor's Schedule C
   business** at fair rental value.
3. **Rental income reported on Schedule E** (not subject to SE
   tax under §1402(a)(1)).
4. **Schedule C income reduced by the rent expense**, lowering
   SE tax base.

### Mechanics

1. **Title the building in spouse's name** (or jointly; recommend
   spouse-only ownership for clarity).
2. **Spouse leases the building to the Schedule C business** at
   FMV under written lease.
3. **Schedule C deducts rent** as "Rent or lease — Other business
   property" (line 20b).
4. **Spouse reports rent on Schedule E** as rental real estate.
5. **Rental expenses** (depreciation, mortgage interest, taxes,
   insurance, repairs) deducted on Schedule E against rental
   income.
6. **No SE tax** on Schedule E income.

### Example

| Without strategy | With strategy |
|---|---|
| Schedule C net income: $100,000 | Schedule C net income (after $24K rent): $76,000 |
| SE tax: ~$14,130 | SE tax: ~$10,738 |
| | Schedule E income: $24,000 (offset by depreciation, etc.) |
| | **SE tax savings: ~$3,391** |

### Bobo / CCA 202151005 services-rendered analysis

Per **Treas. Reg. §1.1402(a)-4(c)** and **Bobo v. Commissioner**,
rental real estate income is **excluded from SE tax** unless
"services rendered for the occupant" are **substantial** and
similar to those of a hotel/motel.

**Excluded from SE (Schedule E):**
- Heat, light, water, trash, parking lot maintenance — these
  are services provided to **maintain the property in condition
  for occupancy**, not "services to the occupant."
- Standard janitorial / common-area maintenance.

**Subject to SE (Schedule C):**
- Daily maid service.
- Concierge services.
- Guest meals.
- Substantial linen / laundry services for occupants.

For a building rented to the spouse's Schedule C business as
office space, no occupant services are typically provided — this
is straightforward Schedule E rental.

### CCA 202151005 (December 23, 2021)

The IRS analyzed two short-term vacation rental scenarios:

**Scenario 1:** Owner provided daily maid service, kitchen
utensils, sundries, Wi-Fi, beach access, recreational equipment,
ride-share vouchers. → **SE tax applies** (substantial services).

**Scenario 2:** Owner provided cleaning between guests,
maintenance to keep property suitable for occupancy. → **No SE
tax** (services are property-maintenance, not occupant-services).

Note: The CCA confirms that **§469 passive activity classification
is irrelevant** for §1402 SE tax purposes. A property may be
"non-passive" under §469 (e.g., short-term rental with material
participation) but still excluded from SE tax under §1402.

### Pitfalls

- **Fair rental value required.** Excessive rent could be
  recharacterized as compensation or as constructive distribution.
  Get a fair market value appraisal or comparable lease
  benchmarks.
- **Self-rental rules under §469(h)(5).** Income from renting
  property to one's own active business is recharacterized as
  active (non-passive) for §469 purposes — generally helpful
  because it avoids passive loss limitation, but creates
  complications if losses exist.
- **Spousal income tax — joint return.** No income tax savings;
  the net rental income still ends up on the joint Form 1040.
  The strategy targets **SE tax** savings.
- **Spouse must have legitimate ownership.** Title transfer to
  spouse must be legitimate (gift, purchase, etc.). Sham
  transfers can be unwound by IRS.
- **Estate planning interaction.** Assets in spouse's name
  receive different estate treatment than jointly-owned assets.
- **State income tax conformity** — most states follow federal
  treatment; some states impose franchise / business activity
  taxes on rental that may complicate.

### Cross-references

- **C6** (Augusta rule — rent home to corp)
- **D1, D3** (spouse-related strategies)

## D5. Home Office Expenses (Schedule C Version)

**Authority:**

- **IRC §280A** (home office requirements)
- **IRC §280A(c)(1)** (exclusive use, regular use, principal
  place of business OR meeting clients OR separate structure)
- **IRC §280A(c)(1) flush language** (administrative or
  management activities — codified post-Soliman expansion)
- **Commissioner v. Soliman**, 506 U.S. 168 (1993) (pre-1999;
  partially superseded by 1997 statutory expansion)
- **Treas. Reg. §1.280A**
- **Form 8829** (Expenses for Business Use of Your Home)
- **Rev. Proc. 2013-13** (simplified method — $5 per sq ft, 300
  sq ft max = $1,500 max)
- **Pub. 587** (Business Use of Your Home)

### Purpose

For Schedule C taxpayers, the home office deduction:

1. **Converts personal expenses to business deductions** —
   utilities, insurance, repairs, depreciation, rent (if
   renting).
2. **Eliminates commuting** — if the home office is the
   **principal place of business**, trips between home and
   client/vendor sites are deductible business mileage (not
   nondeductible commuting).
3. **Reduces SE tax base** — home office deduction reduces
   Schedule C net income, lowering SE tax.

### Eligibility (§280A(c)(1))

The home office must be:

1. **Exclusively used** for business (no personal use of the
   space).
2. **Regularly used** for business (consistent, ongoing use).
3. **One of the following**:
   - **Principal place of business** (where the most important
     activities are conducted, OR where administrative /
     management activities are conducted and there is no other
     fixed location for those activities — per §280A(c)(1) flush
     language enacted in 1997).
   - **Place to meet clients/customers** in the normal course of
     trade.
   - **Separate structure** not attached to the home (e.g.,
     studio, workshop, detached garage).

### Soliman post-1999 statutory fix

Pre-1999, *Commissioner v. Soliman* required the home office to
be the location where the **most important** activities of the
trade were performed. Many service-business taxpayers (doctors,
salesmen, consultants) failed because they performed services at
client sites.

In 1997, Congress added §280A(c)(1) flush language permitting
the home office to qualify as principal place of business if:

- It is used for **administrative or management activities** of
  the trade, AND
- There is **no other fixed location** where the taxpayer
  conducts substantial administrative or management activities.

Effective for tax years beginning after December 31, 1998. This
**legislatively overruled** Soliman and dramatically expanded
home office eligibility for service businesses.

### Two methods of computation

**1. Actual expense method (Form 8829)**

Compute business-use percentage:

- **Square footage method**: Office sq ft / Total home sq ft.
- **Number of rooms method**: Office rooms / Total rooms (only
  if rooms are similar size).

Apply percentage to:

- Mortgage interest (deduct only business share on Schedule C;
  remainder on Schedule A)
- Property tax (same)
- Utilities (electric, gas, water, sewer, trash)
- Insurance
- HOA dues
- Repairs / maintenance allocable to whole home
- Direct repairs (allocable only to office) — 100%
- Depreciation (39-year nonresidential real property if office;
  27.5-year if dwelling unit otherwise — typically 39-year for
  Schedule C office)

**Limitation:** Home office deduction cannot create a loss
beyond gross income from the business use of the home (§280A(c)(5)).
Excess carries forward.

**2. Simplified method (Rev. Proc. 2013-13)**

- $5 per sq ft
- Up to 300 sq ft = $1,500 maximum
- No depreciation (no recapture on sale)
- No carryover of unused deduction

Choose annually; no permanent commitment.

### Commuting / mileage interaction

When home office is **principal place of business** (per the
§280A(c)(1) flush language post-1999), all trips between home
and:

- Client meetings
- Vendor visits
- Bank, post office, supplier
- Any other business destination

are **deductible business mileage**.

Without home office as principal place of business, trips to a
"main work location" (e.g., a co-working space) are
**nondeductible commuting** (the first and last trip of the day
to the regular work location).

### Pitfalls

- **Exclusive use** is the most common audit issue. The space
  must be used **only** for business — no personal computer use,
  guest beds, exercise equipment, kids' homework area.
- **Regular use** — sporadic / occasional use does not qualify.
- **Recapture** — depreciation taken on home office is subject
  to **unrecaptured §1250 gain** at maximum 25% rate on sale of
  the home. Excludes from §121 exclusion.
- **Square footage** — use exterior dimensions, not just
  interior; exclude common areas.
- **Daycare exception** — §280A(c)(4) permits home office
  deduction for daycare facility without exclusive use
  (regular use only required).
- **Home office while traveling** — primary use must be in the
  home; temporary travel doesn't disqualify.

### Cross-references

- **C5 / Part A** (S corp version — different mechanics
  [reimbursement])
- **D7** (heavy vehicle + home office combo)
- **C6** (Augusta rule — separate strategy)

## D6. De Minimis Fringe Benefits `[§132(e)]`

**Authority:**

- **IRC §132(a)(4)** (de minimis fringe excluded from gross
  income)
- **IRC §132(e)** (definition)
- **Treas. Reg. §1.132-6** (de minimis fringe rules)
- **Reg. §1.132-6(c)** (cash generally NOT de minimis — except
  occasional meal money / local transportation fare)
- **Reg. §1.132-6(e)** (examples of de minimis fringes)
- **Notice 2011-72** (cell phones — see C10/D10)

### Purpose

Provide tax-free fringe benefits to employees (including
yourself, if Schedule C with employees) that are individually
small but collectively meaningful, and deduct the cost on
Schedule C.

### What qualifies as de minimis (Reg. §1.132-6(e))

- **Occasional flowers, fruit, books, similar property** for
  special circumstances (illness, family emergency, holiday).
- **Occasional theater / sporting event tickets** (note: TCJA
  may have changed this — verify per Reg. §1.132-6 current).
- **Occasional employee parties / picnics / outings** (also
  100% deductible under §274(e)(4)).
- **Coffee, donuts, soft drinks** in the office.
- **Occasional taxi fares** for working late.
- **Occasional supper money** (meal allowance for working
  overtime).
- **Personal use of office equipment** — copier, printer for
  occasional personal use.
- **Holiday gifts** of low value (turkey, ham, fruit basket — NOT
  cash or cash-equivalent).

### What does NOT qualify

- **Cash** (other than occasional meal money or local
  transportation fare) — not de minimis even if small (Reg.
  §1.132-6(c)).
- **Cash-equivalent gift cards** — generally not de minimis,
  even small amounts (PMTA, IRS audit guidance).
- **Season tickets** (regularly recurring).
- **Memberships** (gym, country club).
- **Use of company vehicle** (other than de minimis personal
  use under specific rules).

### Mechanics for Schedule C

1. **Identify de minimis fringes** for self and any employees.
2. **Pay or provide** the items.
3. **Deduct on Schedule C** under "Office expense" (line 18) or
   "Employee benefit programs" (line 14) as appropriate.
4. **No W-2 income** to recipients.
5. **No FICA / Medicare** withholding.

### Pitfalls

- **Frequency matters** — "occasional" is the key word.
  Recurring or routine benefits are NOT de minimis.
- **Cash and cash equivalents** are excluded from de minimis
  treatment (with narrow exceptions for occasional meal money).
- **Holiday gifts NOT cash** — physical gift (turkey) excluded;
  same value in cash or gift card NOT excluded.
- **Form 1099-MISC reporting** — generally not required for de
  minimis fringes paid to non-employees because of the small
  amount; verify per facts.

### Cross-references

- **C10 / D10** (cell phone — de minimis fringe for personal
  use)
- **§274(e)(4)** (employee party fully deductible)

## D8. Domestic Travel — Majority-of-Days Rule

**Authority:**

- **IRC §162(a)(2)** (away from home for business)
- **IRC §274(d)** (substantiation)
- **Treas. Reg. §1.162-2** (travel expenses generally)
- **Treas. Reg. §1.274-5** (substantiation)
- **Commissioner v. Flowers**, 326 U.S. 465 (1946) (away-from-
  home test)
- **Rev. Proc. 2019-48** (per diem rules)

### Purpose

Convert mixed business/personal travel into deductible business
travel by satisfying the **majority-of-days** test for U.S.
domestic destinations.

### Rule

For domestic travel (within the 50 states + D.C.), if the trip is
**primarily for business**, the **transportation costs to and
from the destination are 100% deductible** even if some days are
personal. "Primarily for business" is generally satisfied when
the **majority of days** are business days.

### Day classification

- **Business days**:
  - Days primarily devoted to business.
  - Travel days (departure and return).
  - Weekend / holiday days SANDWICHED between business days
    (Saturday between Friday and Monday business days).
  - Days on which the taxpayer reasonably expected to do business
    but did not (illness, weather, scheduled-but-cancelled
    meeting).

- **Personal days**:
  - Days primarily devoted to personal activities (sightseeing,
    leisure, family time).

### What's deductible

| Expense | Deductible? |
|---|---|
| Round-trip airfare / transportation | 100% if majority of days are business |
| Lodging (business days only) | 100% |
| Lodging (personal days) | 0% |
| Meals (business days, 50% limit under §274(n)) | 50% |
| Meals (personal days) | 0% |
| Local transportation on business days | 100% |
| Local transportation on personal days | 0% |
| Conference / seminar fees | 100% |

### Substantiation under §274(d)

Strict requirements:

1. **Amount** of each separate expense
2. **Time** (date and duration)
3. **Place** (city, hotel, restaurant)
4. **Business purpose**

Cohan rule **does not apply** — substantiation cannot be
estimated (Sherman v. Comm'r, T.C. Memo 2023-63).

### Spouse / family travel

Per **§274(m)(3)**, spouse or family member travel deductible
only if:

1. Spouse / family member is a **bona fide employee**.
2. Travel has a **business purpose**.
3. Expense would otherwise be deductible.

Otherwise, spouse / family travel is non-deductible. The
proprietor's own incremental cost (vs. solo) is fully deductible
(e.g., if hotel cost is the same single or double, no addition;
if spouse adds a fee, that fee is non-deductible).

### Pitfalls

- **Receipts required for $75+** (Reg. §1.274-5(c)(2)(iii)) and
  all lodging.
- **Mileage log** for any vehicle use during the trip.
- **"Vacation with a business meeting on Day 1"** strategy
  generally fails — must be **primarily for business**.
- **Conference outside North American area** subject to
  additional reasonableness test under §274(h).
- **Cruise ship business travel** — §274(h)(2) limits ($2K/year,
  certain ship registry, attendance evidence).

### Cross-references

- **C9** (S corp travel reimbursement)
- **D9** (foreign travel)

## D9. Foreign Travel — §274(c) Seven-Day / 25% Rule

**Authority:**

- **IRC §274(c)** (foreign travel allocation rules)
- **IRC §274(c)(2)(A)** (1-week exception)
- **IRC §274(c)(2)(B)** (25% nonbusiness exception)
- **IRC §274(c)(2)(C)** (no substantial control over arrangement)
- **IRC §274(c)(2)(D)** (vacation not a major consideration)
- **Treas. Reg. §1.274-4** (foreign travel)

### Purpose

For travel **outside the United States** in pursuit of trade or
business, certain exceptions to the §274(c) allocation rules
permit **full deduction** of transportation costs even when
substantial personal activity occurs.

### General rule (§274(c))

Transportation costs to/from a foreign destination are
**allocated** between business and nonbusiness based on days. If
4 of 7 days are business: 4/7 of transportation deductible.

### Exceptions (full deduction permitted)

§274(c)(2) provides four exceptions; if **any one** applies, the
entire transportation cost is deductible:

**(A) The trip outside the United States, away from home, does
not exceed one week.**

- "One week" = 7 consecutive days, **not counting** the day of
  departure but **counting** the day of return.
- Example: Travel Sat → return Fri = 6 days = within 1 week.

**(B) The portion of the time of travel outside the United
States, away from home, which is not attributable to the pursuit
of the taxpayer's trade or business is less than 25 percent of
the total time on such travel.**

- Total time = days outside U.S. (including travel days).
- Calculate: nonbusiness days ÷ total days. If < 25%, full
  transportation deductible.
- Example: 8-day trip with 6 business + 2 personal = 25% personal
  → exception fails (must be < 25%, not ≤). Plan to keep
  personal at 24% or less.

**(C) The taxpayer did not have substantial control over
arranging the business trip.**

- Common-law employees of the company (not "managing executives")
  who don't control trip timing/itinerary.
- 10%+ owners are deemed to have substantial control.
- Rarely applicable to small business owners.

**(D) The taxpayer can establish that obtaining a personal
vacation or holiday was not a major consideration in determining
to make the trip.**

- Burden on taxpayer to establish; subjective test; rarely
  available.

### Worked examples (adapted from Reg. §1.274-4(g))

**Example 1 — 1-week exception applies:**

Taxpayer flies New York → Paris on Monday for 1 day of business.
Returns Wednesday after 2 days of personal sightseeing.

- Days: Mon (depart) + Tue (business) + Wed (return) = 3 days,
  + 2 personal days = 5 days total away from home.
- 5 days < 7 days → **§274(c)(2)(A) 1-week exception applies**.
- **100% transportation deductible**.

**Example 2 — 25% rule applies:**

Taxpayer flies New York → London for 8 days. 6 days business,
2 days vacation.

- 2 personal / 8 total = 25% → exception **fails** (must be < 25%).
- §274(c) general allocation: 6/8 of transportation = 75%
  deductible. Lodging/meals on personal days = 0% deductible.

**Example 3 — Plan for 7 personal of 28-day trip:**

Taxpayer plans 28 days abroad with 21 business + 7 personal.

- 7 / 28 = 25% → exception fails by exactly 25%.
- Better: 21 business + 6 personal (27 days total). 6/27 = 22.2%
  < 25% → **§274(c)(2)(B) exception applies**, 100%
  transportation deductible.

### Foreign convention (§274(h))

Conventions held outside the **North American area** must
satisfy a separate reasonableness test under §274(h):

- The convention is **as reasonable** to be held outside the
  North American area as within it.
- Factors: location of attendees, reasons for foreign location,
  purposes of organizing entity.

The North American area includes the U.S., its possessions,
Trust Territory of the Pacific Islands, Canada, Mexico, and
specified Caribbean and Pacific countries listed in Rev. Proc.
2007-66 (verify current list).

### Pitfalls

- **Day-counting precision** is critical. A single day's
  miscalculation can cost the entire transportation deduction.
- **§274(d) substantiation** still applies — receipts, time,
  place, purpose.
- **Foreign exchange rates** — convert at appropriate exchange
  rate (date of expense).
- **Cohan unavailable** — strict substantiation.
- **No "1-week + 1-day" deduction** — exact 7-day count fails;
  the trip must NOT EXCEED one week.

## D10. Cell Phone

**Cross-reference:** See **C10** for full treatment. Mechanics are
identical for Schedule C; the only difference is the deduction
location:

- **S corp**: Form 1120-S Other Deductions (line 19) or
  reimbursed as accountable plan
- **Schedule C**: Schedule C "Office expense" (line 18) or
  "Other expenses" (line 27a)

### Schedule C application

For Schedule C taxpayers:

1. **Business cell phone owned by the business** — fully
   deductible; if also used personally, allocate (or apply
   Notice 2011-72 if primarily for business).
2. **Personal cell phone used for business** — deduct
   business-use percentage.
3. **Phone provided to employees** primarily for noncompensatory
   business reasons → working condition fringe + de minimis
   fringe (no W-2 income).
